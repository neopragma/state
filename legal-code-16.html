Tax Law

16. Tax Law.
This section of the legal code concerns tax law.

16.1. Definitions.

16.1.1. Tax. A monetary charge on persons or property payable to the Government.
CR: Article 6.1

16.1.2. Income. Money that comes in to an individual or organization, regardless of the mechanism. This includes wages, salaries, and contracting fees for work performed; interest earned on investments; rent collected from tenants; revenue from sales; and anything of a similar nature.

16.1.3. Income Tax. A tax on income charged to individuals and organizations.

16.1.4. Capital Gain. The difference between the sale price and the purchase price of an asset.

16.1.5. Capital Gains Tax. A tax on the amount earned through the sale of an asset.

16.1.6. Value Added. An increase in the value of a transaction due to handling or improvements to an item between its manufacture and its final point of sale.

16.1.7. Value Added Tax (VAT), or Sales Tax. A tax levied on sales transactions.

16.1.8. Excise, or Excise Tax. A tax or fee levied on businesses in the supply chain to cover the cost of infrastructure, based on the use of that infrastructure.

16.1.9. Ad Valorum Tax. An excise tax based on a fixed percentage rate, usually defined for specific goods or services.

16.1.10. Specific Tax. An excise tax based on a specific amount for particular types of purchases.

16.2. Tax Accounting. These statutes apply to all forms of tax except where otherwise noted.

16.2.1. Tax Accounting Period. Records affecting tax liability will be summarized and reported, and payments to and from the State will be due, on an annual basis from the first day of a calendar year until the last day of the same year.

16.2.2. Tax Reporting and Payment Period. Reporting of accounting information and payments to and from the State must be completed for the preceding tax accounting period within three months of the end of that period. That is, taxpayers have until March 31 of each year to close their tax obligations for the preceding calendar year.

16.2.3. Tax Reporting and Payment Assistance.

16.2.3.1. The Government is directed to facilitate taxpayer accounting, reporting, and payment procedures; to provide timely, accurate, and complete information pertinent to tax liability, payment, and relief; and offer direct assistance to taxpayers to minimize errors and confusion.

16.2.3.2. The Government may charge taxpayers a service fee for direct assistance in an amount not to exceed a maximum amount to be set at the regulatory level.

16.2.3.3. Conflicts arising from errors or omissions in Government-provided information and assistance are to be resolved in favor of the taxpayer. Procedures for conflict resolution are to be set at the regulatory level.

16.3. Errors in Tax Calculations or Payments.

16.3.1. Absent fraudulent intent, an error in calculating taxes due or refunds due will not be deemed an offense.

16.3.2. The Government or a taxpayer may detect an error in a past tax calculation or payment through a review of their own records, public Government records (by a taxpayer), or a taxpayer audit (by the Government).

16.3.3. An error detected in the most recent tax report or payment (pertaining to the immediately-previous year) may be corrected in the next tax reporting period.

16.3.4. An error detected in an older tax report or payment (before the immediately-previous year) may be corrected through a series of payments to be agreed between the taxpayer and the Government, subject to relevant regulations.

16.3.5. If the Government detects an error in a prior year's tax report or payment by a taxpayer that requires payment from the taxpayer to the Government for correction, the Government must notify the taxpayer within five (5) years of the tax reporting deadline in which the error was made.

16.3.6. If anyone detects an error in a prior year's tax report or payment that requires payment from the Government to a taxpayer for correction, the taxpayer may notify the Government at any time; there is no statute of limitations.

16.3.7. Corrective payments between the Government and a taxpayer may be made in a lump sum or in a series of instalments, subject to relevant regulations.

16.3.8. Neither the Government nor taxpayers will be subject to penalties or interest when making corrective payments due to honest errors.

16.4. Tax Fraud. When a taxpayer intentionally misrepresents their tax liability in official tax reporting documents in order to reduce their tax liability, the matter is considered tax fraud.

16.4.1. Tax fraud committed by an individual or household will be treated as a Category 2 offense. Offenders will not be subject to incarceration.

16.4.2. Tax fraud that has no implications for the Three Pillars of Independence, when committed by the leaders of or on behalf of an organization, will be treated as a Category 2 offense. Individual offenders will not be subject to incarceration.

16.4.3. Tax fraud that has negative implications for the Three Pillars of Independence, when committed by the leaders of or on behalf of an organization, will be treated as a Category 1 offense because of the effect on the Three Pillars, and not specifically because the offense is related to taxation. Penalties will be determined in the usual way for Category 1 offenses. Incarceration is possible depending on factors other than the tax fraud that may pertain to the case.

16.5. Taxpayer Categories.

16.5.1. Organizations.

16.5.1.1. Organizations are subject to various forms of taxation at rates set at the regulatory level.

16.5.1.2. Organizations that are registered as providing a valuable service to society may be eligible for income tax relief in accordance with relevant regulations.

16.5.2. Individuals. Individual citizens and non-citizens of majority age are subject to Income Tax and Value-Add Tax at rates set at the regulatory level.

16.5.2.1. Individual citizens and non-citizens of majority age are subject to Income Tax and Value-Add Tax at rates set at the regulatory level.

16.5.2.2. Individual citizens who reside in a foreign country and are subject to income taxation in that country will not be subject to the Basic Income Tax during their residency in the other country.

16.5.3. Households.

16.5.3.1. By unanimous agreement, members of a household may elect to calculate, report, and pay income taxes as a unit rather than separately as individuals. They may change this election at the next or any subsequent tax reporting deadline. Any individual adult member of the household may terminate the agreement.

16.5.3.2. When a household elects to pay taxes as a unit, the opt-in or opt-out choices for optional income taxes apply to each adult member of the household.

16.5.3.3. An individual who withdraws from a household that is paying taxes as a unit must calculate, report, and pay income taxes as an individual for the entire tax year in which they withdrew from the household.

16.5.3.4. When a household that is paying taxes as a unit dissolves, the former members may elect to pay taxes for the current year collectively or individually. Unanimous consent of the former members is required to qualify for collective payment. This option does not apply to households that were not already paying as a unit at the time they dissolved the household.

16.6. Income Taxes.

16.6.1. Income. All forms of income are treated the same (payroll, capital gains, investment interest, rents, etc.).

16.6.2. Income Tax Categories.

16.6.2.1. Basic Income Tax. All taxpayers are subject to the Basic Income Tax, except as provided in 16.5.1.2 and 16.5.2.2.

16.6.2.2. Healthcare Tax.

16.6.2.2.1. Optional Tax. Individuals, as well as households paying as a unit, may choose to pay a tax on income that covers the costs of the national healthcare system. Those who opt in are eligible to receive healthcare services through the national system.

16.6.2.2.2. Opt-In and Opt-Out. Taxpayers may elect to pay or not to pay the Healthcare Tax, and change their election, once per year when they file their annual tax report.

16.6.2.2.3. Citizenship Level. Taxpayers who opt out of the Healthcare Tax and healthcare system are reduced to Basic Citizen status.

16.6.2.3. Pension Tax.

16.6.2.3.1. Optional Tax. Individuals, as well as households paying as a unit, may choose to pay a tax on income that covers the costs of the national pension system. Those who opt in are eligible to receive a pension after they reach the standard retirement age as defined at the regulatory level.

16.6.2.3.2. Opt-In and Opt-Out. Taxpayers may elect to pay or not to pay the Pension Tax, and change their election, once per year when they file their annual tax report. The amount of the pension will be calculated based on the number of years an individual paid the Pension Tax during their lifetime.

16.6.2.3.3. Citizenship Level. Taxpayers who opt out of the Pension Tax are reduced to Basic Citizen status.

16.6.2.4. Guaranteed Minimum Income Tax.

16.6.2.4.1. Optional Tax. Individuals, as well as households paying as a unit, may choose to pay a tax on income that covers the costs of the Guaranteed Minimum Income benefit. Those who opt in are eligible to receive Guaranteed Minimum Income payments.

16.6.2.4.2. Opt-In and Opt-Out. Taxpayers may elect to pay or not to pay the Guaranteed Minimum Income Tax, and change their election, once per year when they file their annual tax report.

16.6.2.4.3. Citizenship Level. Taxpayers who opt out of the Guaranteed Minimum Income Tax are reduced to Basic Citizen status.

16.6.2.5. Excess Income Exemptions.

16.6.2.5.1. Excess Income Account. Excess individual income that is deposited into the Government-managed excess income account will not be subject to income tax unless and until it is withdrawn later.

16.6.2.5.2. Excess Income Transfer. Excess individual income that is transferred to an organization will be treated as taxable income to the organization.

16.6.2.5.3. Pension payments are not treated as taxable income.
