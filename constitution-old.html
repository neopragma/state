The Constitution of the State

1. Purpose
This section defines the purpose of the State's existence.

1.1. Fundamental Values
The State exists for the purpose of defending and upholding the values, rights, and practical considerations documented in this section.

1.1.1. Human Needs
The State will establish and sustain the conditions necessary for the people to satisfy their human needs, as defined in the Addendum, Human Needs

1.1.2. Human Rights
The State will unconditionally defend and uphold human rights, as defined in the Addendum, Human Rights.

1.1.3. Legal Rights
The State will defend and uphold legal rights, as defined in the Addendum, Legal Rights, on condition that people conform with the laws of the State. Violation of law will carry penalties as defined in the Legal Code. Such penalties may include limitation of specific legal rights for offenders.

1.1.4. Sustainable Independence
The State will set standards, provide incentives, monitor compliance, and levy punishment as appropriate to ensure the country can operate independently in three key areas, known as the Three Pillars of Independence.

1.1.4.1. Food Independence
Food Independence means the country can feed its people indefinitely without dependencies on external assistance.

1.1.4.2. Energy Independence
Energy Independence means the country can provide its own energy needs indefinitely without dependencies on external sources.

1.1.4.3. Defense Independence
Defense Independence means the country can support its own national defense indefinitely without dependencies on external assistance.

2. Structure of Government
This section describes the structure of the government.

2.1. Single National Government
The State is organized as a single national government, without autonomous subdivisions or members.

2.1.1. Regional and Local State Organs
The State may establish regional and/or local organs to carry out the State's responsibilities in cases when practical considerations warrant it. These organs are extensions of the State, and are not autonomous entities.

2.1.2. Autonomous Local Organizations
The people who live in a given area may form their own quasi-governmental organizations to help them manage affairs in their area.

2.1.2.1. Local Rules
Local quasi-governmental organizations can institute rules that the people in their area agree to follow, but they cannot pass laws with the same force as State laws.

2.1.2.2. Local Taxation
Local quasi-governmental organizations can levy taxes, charge fees for services, and issue bonds, subject to the approval of the people they serve.

2.1.2.3. Limits of Power
Local quasi-governmental organizations do not have the same power as State organs.

2.1.2.3.1. No Override of State Law
Local quasi-governmental organizations are free to manage any and all business in the area they were created to serve, except they may under no circumstances subvert the Three Pillars of Independence or establish local rules that contravene human rights or the legal rights defined at the national level, or deprive people of human needs. Should they do so, they will be subject to State intervention.

2.1.2.3.2. Limits on Incarceration
Local quasi-governmental organizations can detain individuals temporarily for minor offenses such as disorderly conduct, but they cannot conduct trials or operate prisons. Detained individuals cannot be required to carry out any work.

2.1.2.4. Termination or Removal
Local quasi-governmental organizations operate at the pleasure of the people they serve. Should the people desire to remove or reform an organization that exerts power over them, and the organization refuses, the people may ask the State to step in to resolve the conflict.

2.2. Decision Making Council
The Decision Making Council (DMC) is responsible for decisions that affect, or may potentially affect, the Three Pillars of Independence. The DMC is the primary decision-making body of the State.

2.2.1. Size
The DMC comprises fifteen (15) individuals.

2.2.2. Qualifications
To qualify for membership on the DMC, an individual must meet all the following requirements.

2.2.2.1. Full Citizenship
DMC members must have Full Citizenship status. If for any reason a member loses Full Citizenship status during their term, they are immediately removed from the DMC.

2.2.2.1. National Advisory Board service
DMC members must have served a minimum of five (5) years on the National Advisory Board (NAB). The service need not be continuous or current. During the Initial Establishment period, this requirement is adjusted according to the terms set forth in item 2.4.

2.2.3. Election of Members
Members are elected every five (5) years in groups of three (3). The three members whose term limits have expired must retire from the DMC. During the Initial Establishment period, this requirement is adjusted according to the terms set forth in item 2.4.

2.2.3.1. Election Process
DMC members are elected by the current members of the National Advisory Board. A slate of candidates is generated from a combination of (a) NAB member nominations, (b) DMC member nominations, and (c) citizen petition. NAB and DMC members may nominate a maximum of three (3) individuals per election. Citizen petitions must be signed by enough people for a minimum of 20,000 voting points. A citizen petition may be used to submit one candidate. Multiple citizen petitions are allowed, with no maximum number. NAB members mark a vote of confidence for each candidate. The three candidates who win the most votes of confidence are elected.

2.2.3.2. Special Elections
In the event a member leaves the DMC prior to the end of their five-year term, a special election is held to replace them. The person so elected completes the original term of the member who vacated. If the remaining term amounts to less than one (1) calendar year, the replacement member need not wait before standing for election again, per item 2.2.5. If no NAB member wishes to stand for the replacement post, the DMC may appoint someone from the NAB by majority vote.

2.2.4. Term Limits
The maximum contiguous time an individual can serve on the DMC is five (5) years. After that, the individual must wait for the defined waiting period before standing for election to the DMC again.

2.2.5. Waiting Period for Reelection
After leaving the DMC for any reason, an individual must wait five (5) years before standing again for election.

2.2.6. Age Limit
Individuals sixty-six (66) years of age or older may not stand for election to the DMC. A member who reaches their 66th birthday while on the DMC can finish the remainder of their term.

2.2.7. Operating Procedures
The members of the DMC define the operating procedures for the DMC.

2.3. National Advisory Board
The National Advisory Board (NAB) provides analytical and informational support to the DMC, provides advice to the DMC, and reviews the decisions of the DMC for compliance with the State's fundamental values and purpose.

2.3.1. Size
The NAB comprises one thousand (1,000) individuals.

2.3.2. Qualifications
To qualify for membership on the NAB, an individual must meet all the following requirements.

2.3.2.1. Full Citizenship
NAB members must have Full Citizenship status. If for any reason a member loses Full Citizenship status during their term, they are immediately removed from the NAB.

2.3.2.2. Higher Education
NAB members must have completed formal education at least through the level of Bachelor's Degree. A wide diversity of subject matter expertise is desired.

2.3.3. Election of Members
Members are elected every two (2) years in groups of one hundred (100). The hundred members whose term limits have expired must retire from the NAB. During the Initial Establishment period, this requirement is adjusted according to the terms set forth in item 2.4.

2.3.4. Term Limits
The maximum contiguous time an individual can belong to the NAB is fifteen (15) years. After that, the individual must wait for the defined waiting period before standing for election to the NAB again.

2.3.5. Waiting Period for Reelection
After leaving the NAB for any reason, an individual must wait five (5) years before standing again for election.

2.3.6. Age Limit
There is no age limit for service on the NAB.

2.3.7. Operating Procedures
The members of the NAB define the operating procedures for the NAB.

2.4. Initial Establishment
The first fifteen (15) years of State operation is called the Initial Establishment period. During this time, the staggered election process for the DMC and NAB cannot be honored. This subsection specifies the adjustments to the rules that apply during the Initial Establishment period.

2.4.1. Placeholder Authority
The military will serve as the national authority from the beginning of the State until the first elections for the DMC and NAB have been completed. When the initial membership of the NAB has been set according to the procedure outlined in item 2.4.3, the placeholder authority will be dissolved.

2.4.2. DMC Initial Establishment
After a period of three months for campaigning, the first fifteen (15) members of the DMC will be elected by popular vote of Full Citizens. The five (5) individuals receiving the most votes will serve a fifteen (15) year term. The next group of five will serve a ten (10) year term. The five receiving the lowest votes will serve a five (5) year term. This will establish the rotation based on term limits going forward.

2.4.3. NAB Initial Establishment
After the initial DMC has been established, DMC members will nominate candidates for the NAB. Full Citizens can nominate candidates via petition, with 20,000 signatures required per candidate. Based on number of votes garnered, the top set of 100 NAB members will serve fifteen (15) year terms, the next set ten (10), and the next set five (5). This will establish the rotation based on term limits going forward.

3. Citizenship
A citizen enjoys certain privileges and accepts certain responsibilities that other residents of the country do not. This section outlines the criteria for obtaining and maintaining citizenship and the rights and responsibilities associated with citizenship.

3.1. Obtaining Citizenship
This section outlines the ways in which an individual can become a citizen.

3.1.1. Natural Citizenship
An individual is considered a natural citizen when either of the following criteria is true:

3.1.1.1. Citizenship by Parentage
At least one of the individual's biological parents or grandparents is a citizen.

3.1.1.2. Citizenship by Transfer
The individual was a citizen of the United States of America (USA) at the time the State was established, and is not known to have belonged to any subversive or seditionist organization in the USA, or otherwise participated in or supported the seditionist movement(s) of the early 21st century in the USA.

3.1.2. Adoptive Citizenship
An individual is considered an adoptive citizen when they immigrated to the country, followed the State's procedures to apply for citizensip, and were approved.

3.2. Levels of Citizenship
The State defines multiple levels of citizenship, each with particular privileges and responsibilities.

3.2.1. Full Citizenship
Natural and adoptive citizens qualify for Full Citizenship when they are fully invested in the system, as indicated by their election to pay all optional income taxes and participate in all State services.

3.2.2. Basic Citizenship
Natural and adoptive citizens qualify for Basic Citizenship when they opt out of one or more State services.

3.2.3. Probationary Citizenship
An individual with probationary citizenship is in the process of demonstrating willingness to abide by the laws of the State. There are three situations in which an individual is considered a probationary citizen:

3.2.3.1. Currently Serving Sentence
An individual who is serving a sentence for a criminal conviction has Probationary Citizenship status.

3.2.3.2. In Process At Time of Transfer
An individual who was in the process of becoming a US citizen at the time the State was established will continue the process of applying for citizenship under the State's laws.

3.2.3.3. Possible Seditionist At Time of Transfer
An individual who belonged to a seditionist organization or otherwise participated in or supported sedition in the US prior to the establishment of the State is considered to be in probationary citizenship status pending their demonstration of willingness to abide by the State's laws.

3.3. Citizenship and Voting
Full and Basic Citizens have the privilege and the obligation to vote. Voting is not optional.

3.3.1. Full Citizenship and Voting
Full Citizens can vote on the following matters:

3.3.1.1. Consequence
Full Citizens may vote on any matter except those that affect, or may potentially affect, the Three Pillars of Independence.

3.3.1.2. Representation
Full Citizens may vote on nominations to the National Advisory Board.

3.3.1.2. Petitions
Full Citizens may circulate and sign petitions to the State.

3.3.1.3. Education and Voting Strength
Anyone who has completed formal education to the level of Bachelor's Degree or higher is considered "educated" for purposes of this subsection. Others are considered "vocational" for purposes of this subsection.

3.3.1.3.1. Educated Full Citizen Voting Strength
Each vote and each signature on a petition counts as three (3).

3.3.1.3.2. Vocational Full Citizen Voting Strength
Each vote and each signature on a petition counts as two (2).

3.3.2. Basic Citizenship and Voting
This subsection outlines the voting rights and obligations of Basic Citizens.

3.3.2.1. Consequence
Basic Citizens can vote on matters of local and regional consequence.

3.3.2.2. Basic Citizenship and Petitions
Basic Citizens can circulate and sign petitions.

3.3.2.3 Basic Citizen Voting Strength
Each vote cast and each signature on a petition counts as one (1).

3.4. Changes in Citizenship Status
Citizenship status may change due to any one of the follow situations:

3.4.1. Opting Out of Investment in the System
When an individual with Full Citizenship opts out of tax-paid services, their citizenship level becomes Basic.

3.4.2. Opting Into Investment in the System
When an individual with Basic Citizenship opts into all tax-paid services, their citizenship level becomes Full.

3.4.3. Completion of Sentence
When an individual completes a sentence for a criminal conviction, their status changes from Probationary to Basic Citizenship.

3.4.4. Criminal Conviction
When an individual is convicted of a crime, their citizenship status becomes Probationary.

3.4.5. Foreign Agency
When an individual holds citizenship in the State as well as one or more foreign countries, and any one of the foreign countries is in conflict with the State, the individual will be reduced to Probationary Citizenship.

3.5. Loss of Citizenship
This subsection concerns loss of citizenship.

3.5.1. Voluntary Renunciation of Citizenship
Any individual is free to renounce their citizenship at any time and for any reason. This is considered an expression of opinion and does not permanently remove eligibility for citizenship. The individual may recover their citizenship upon request.

3.5.2. Involuntary Removal of Citizenship
The State is prohibited from removing anyone's citizenship as punishment for a crime or for any other reason.

3.6. Multiple Citizenship
There is no prohibition against holding citizenship with the State as well as with one or more foreign countries.

4. Residency and Visa Categories
Non-citizen residents of the country must hold a valid visa. The details of visa types and the rules regarding residency and work are at the statutory level and are described in the Legal Code.
