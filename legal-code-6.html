Residency and Visas

6. Residency and Visas
This section of the Legal Code concerns the legal status of non-Citizens who are present in the country.
CR: 5.7
BG: Backgrounder on Residency and Visas

6.1. The following categories of legal status pertain to non-Citizens who are present in America.

  • Resident - long-term or permanent residence in America, with work privileges
  • Visitor - fixed-term presence in America for a defined purpose, with or without work privileges
  • Diplomat - individual assigned to diplomatic service of a foreign government, posted in America
  • Military - foreign military personnel assigned to America for (a) training, (b) joint exercises, (c) consultation, or (d) post of service
  • Refugee - individual temporarily in America as a refugee from abroad
  • Asylum - individual temporarily in America due to political risk in their country of origin
  • Undocumented - individual in America without any legal status

6.2. Visa Types
This section of the Legal Code defines the types of visa required for each category of non-Citizen presence in America.

6.2.1. Resident Visa
This visa type is for a non-Citizen whose primary place of residence is in America.

6.2.1.1. The Resident Visa has no defined expiration date, but must be renewed annually for record-keeping purposes. The State will respect a good-faith effort to renew, provided such renewal is initiated within one calendar year of the scheduled renewal date. Further delay may be grounds to rescind Resident status, at the discretion of the State or of a court.

6.2.1.2. A holder of a Resident Visa has work privileges, but cannot work (a) in government, (b) in law enforcement, (c) as a commissioned military officer.

6.2.1.3. A holder of a Resident Visa may opt in or out of optional income taxes and their associated social services.

6.2.1.4. A holder of a Resident Visa may not vote, petition the government, or serve on a jury.

6.2.1.5. A holder of a Resident Visa may participate in lawful public protests.

6.2.1.6. A court or the State may rescind Resident status if the holder of the visa is convicted of a crime, at the discretion of the court or the State.

6.2.1.7. A holder of a Resident Visa may apply for Citizenship provided they (a) have resided primarily in America for five consecutive years as of the date of application, and (b) have had no Category 1 or Category 2 criminal convictions during that five-year period. The term "primarily" is intentionally vague, with the intent that desirable individuals should not be hampered by details such as the exact number of days or weeks they were in America.

6.2.2. Fixed-Term Visa
This visa type is intended for visitors to America, such as tourists, international employee, temporary employee, guest workers, seasonal workers, students, media, religious worker, crewmember, and transit during international travel.

6.2.2.1. The term of the stay is set at the time the visa is issued and is based on the purpose of the stay.

6.2.2.2. Eligibility to work, including the type(s) of employment permitted, is determined at the time the visa is issued and is based on the purpose of the stay.

6.2.2.3. The holder may apply for one extension without penalty, in case they are unable to leave America on time for reasons beyond their control.

6.2.2.4. The holder does not pay taxes and is not eligible for social services except emergency medical care.

6.2.2.5. A holder of a Fixed-Term Visa may not vote, petition the government, serve on a jury, or participate in public protests.

6.2.2.6. A holder of a Fixed-Term Visa may apply for Resident status while in America, provided the term of the visa has not expired.

6.2.2.7. A holder of a Fixed-Term Visa may apply for political asylum while in America, provided the term of the visa has not expired.

6.2.2.8. A holder of a Fixed-Term Visa may not apply for Citizenship.

6.2.2.9. The State will honor a one-month grace period for those who overstay the term of their visa.

6.2.2.10. A holder of a Fixed-Term Visa who overstays the term of the visa and makes no good-faith effort to extend the term may be barred from re-entering America for a period of one calendar year from the date their status is discovered by the authorities, at the discretion of the State or of a court.

6.2.3. Diplomatic Visa
This visa type is intended for individuals who have been posted to America on diplomatic assignment by a foreign government.

6.2.3.1. A Diplomatic Visa has no defined end date, but must be renewed annually on a timely basis.

6.2.3.2. A holder of a Diplomatic Visa has no eligibility to work in America beyond their diplomatic duties.

6.2.3.3. A holder of a Diplomatic Visa does not pay income taxes and is not eligible for social services except emergency medical care.

6.2.3.4. A holder of a Diplomatic Visa enjoys diplomatic immunity as that term is commonly understood, with the exception of Category 1 crimes.

6.2.3.5. Others who reside with the diplomat, such as family members, have no diplomatic status. Each must apply for a Fixed-Term Visa and abide by the rules associated with that visa type.

6.2.3.6. The State may rescind a Diplomatic Visa at its discretion.

6.2.4. Military Visa
This visa type is for foreign military personnel assigned by their government to America for training, joint exercises, consultation, or as their duty station.

6.2.4.1. A Military Visa has no defined end date, but must be renewed annually on a timely basis.

6.2.4.2. A holder of a Military Visa has no eligibility to work in America beyond their military duties.

6.2.4.3. A holder of a Military Visa does not pay income taxes and is not eligible for social services except emergency medical care.

6.2.4.4. Foreign military codes do not supersede American law on American soil. A holder of a Military Visa must conform with American laws.

6.2.4.5. Others who reside with the holder of a Military Visa, such as family members, have no military status. Each must apply for a Fixed-Term Visa and abide by the rules associated with that visa type.

6.2.4.6. The State may rescind a Military Visa at its discretion.

6.3. Undocumented Presence
This section of the Legal Code pertains to individuals present in America on any basis other than the visa types defined in this Section.

6.3.1. All persons present in America are entitled to human rights and legal rights, and to emergency medical care.

6.3.2. Anyone present in America without legal status has no eligibility to work.

6.3.3. Anyone who entered America without applying for legal status must apply for status as (a) a visitor, (b) a refugee, or (c) an asylum-seeker within three months of their arrival in America. Violators may be removed from the country at the discretion of the State, and may be barred from re-entry for one calendar year, at the discretion of the State or of a court.

6.3.4. Anyone who entered America legally and then overstayed the term of their visa may be removed from the country at the discretion of the State, and may be barred from re-entry for one calendar year, at the discretion of the State or of a court.

6.4. Criminal Convictions and Visa Status
This section of the Legal Code defines the effect of a criminal conviction on an individual's visa status.

6.4.1. A holder of a Resident Visa who is convicted of a crime will be treated the same as a Citizen, with the additional provision that their Resident status may be rescinded at the discretion of the State or of a court, and they may be removed from the country and barred from re-entry for a period of one calendar year following their release from prison (if incarcerated) or the date of conviction (if not incarcerated) at the discretion of the court at the time of sentencing.

6.4.2. A holder of a Fixed-Term Visa or Military Visa who is convicted of a crime will be removed from the country and barred from re-entry for the greater of (a) one calendar year or (b) the term determined by the court at the time of sentencing, from the date of their release from prison (if incarcerated) or the date of conviction (if not incarcerated).

6.4.3. A holder of a Diplomatic Visa who is convicted of a Category 1 crime will be removed from the country and barred from re-entry permanently.

6.5. Special Circumstances
This section of the Legal Code defines the effect of special circumstances on visa status.

6.5.1. Circumstances that preclude international travel, such as natural disasters, pandemics, and wars, will cause the clock to stop on all visas. The clock is resumed when the State determines the special circumstances have passed. The timing for renewal of Fixed-Term Visas will resume as if no time had passed while the special circumstances prevailed.

6.6. Limitations on Visa Approvals
This section of the Legal Code specifies limitations the State may impose on visa approvals.

6.6.1. The number of visas approved may be adjusted at the discretion of the State to ensure Americans enjoy the maximum opportunity for work and career growth, or conversely to ensure the country has sufficient qualified workers in a given field to support the people's needs.

6.6.2. Individuals whom the State deems "undesirable" may be denied visas at the discretion of the State.

6.6.3. The State may limit the number of refugees and/or asylum-seekers in the country at any given time, to ensure the sustainability of the Three Pillars of Independence and to ensure the country has the economic capability and facilities necessary to enable Americans to receive the rights and benefits of Citizenship and Residency.
