Backgrounder on the Three Pillars of Independence

What conditions must be true for the people to live according to their own values without external interference? The country must be able to provide for its own basic needs for (a) food, (b) energy, and (c) defense on a sustainable basis.

1. Overview

1.1. Effects of Foreign Dependencies on Our Freedom

When one country holds sway over another, through economic hegemony, military threat, or control of a key need such as food, energy, or defense, it is not possible for the government, companies, or individuals in the second country to make decisions freely according to their own values and goals. They must comply with anything the first country demands of them, even when doing so causes them to suffer materially, physically, or emotionally, and to act contrary to their own deeply-held values. In some cases, the second country may be forced to act in a way contrary to its own long-term interests, in order to avoid short-term pain.

To be truly free, we must establish the conditions in our own country that enable us to speak and act in accordance with our own values, without bending to the will of any foreign power.

This is the reason for our firmness regarding the Three Pillars of Independence. The pillars are the sturdy framework that enables the State to uphold individual liberty on a sustainable basis. Such liberty cannot extend to the point that it tears down this framework; otherwise, liberty itself will be subverted. No one's personal freedom in the short term trumps everyone else's freedom in the long term.

Therefore, a company that takes the position of "company over country" may be prosecuted, and an individual who enables foreign interests to gain influence in our country as a way to gain personal wealth may be prosecuted. These prosecutions will be harsher than those for ordinary crimes, because they are not ordinary crimes; they are an existential threat to the country.

1.2. Independence in Food Production, Quality, and Distribution

If our country depends on external sources of food, or food cannot be distributed to those who need it, then those who control the food can dictate that our people conform to their values, deliver whatever they demand, and support their actions, whatever they may be.

Therefore, our people can only be free if they can produce and distribute their own food. In addition, the food must be safe to consume.

This pertains to basic foods, not luxury goods. It means "the people" as a whole, not every individual separately.

The first pillar is Independence in Food Production and Distribution.

1.3. Independence in Energy Production and Distribution

If our country depends on external sources of energy, or energy cannot be distributed to those who need it, then those who control the energy sources can dictate that our people conform to their values, deliver whatever they demand, and support their actions, whatever they may be.

Therefore, our people can only be free if they can generate and distribute their own energy. It means "the people" as a whole, not every individual separately.

The second pillar is Independence in Energy Production and Distribution.

1.4. Independence in National Defense

If our country depends on external support for national defense, then those who provide such support can dictate that our people conform to their values, deliver whatever they demand, and support their actions, whatever they may be.

Therefore, our people can only be free if they can defend the country without dependency on foreign sources of equipment, raw materials, or direct support. It means "the people" as a whole, not every individual separately. This means core homeland defenses, and not necessarily every weapon system deployed in every theater.

The third pillar is Independence in Defense.

2. Critical Success Factors

Certain common success factors apply across the board. These include:

  • decentralized capability in all three areas;
  • robust and dependable interconnectivity among the decentralized sites (transportation, communication, and information infrastructure);
  • systems that operate over a long span of time and that are designed for maintenance, technical updating, and functional improvement (sustainability);
  • systems that can withstand substantial damage without losing functionality to a debilitating extent, and that can be restored to a fully functioning state in a reasonable time and at reasonable cost (resiliency);
  • a population that is educated and well-informed about matters related to the three pillars (education); and
  • consistent, mindful regulation, oversight, and enforcement of laws pertaining to public-sector and private-sector operations relevant to the three pillars (enforcement).

Absent any one of these factors, the system will be unable to serve its purpose.

2.1. Decentralized Capability

If capability in any given area is concentrated in a few places, or if that capability depends on just one or two methods of production or distribution, then a well-planned attack or an unfortunate natural event that strikes just a few key targets has the potential to cripple the country. Therefore, it is important to design decentralized capability across the three pillars.

The concept of decentralization pertains to two aspects of these key activities: Physical distribution of production sites, and the use of multiple methods or techniques for any given production or distribution activity.

For the food pillar, this may mean both (a) multiple geographical areas producing similar foods and multiple locations for collecting, storing, and redistributing water; and (b) multiple agricultural and preparation methods for producing foods and multiple means of transporting foods across the country.

For the energy pillar, it may mean (a) energy production at the point of use, in local and regional grids, and in a national grid, with multiple redundant and severable connections; as well as (b) multiple energy production methods using different raw materials, and multiple methods of transmitting and storing energy.

For the defense pillar, it may mean many more and smaller military bases, each with a range of capabilities and stores rather than a specialized function, and multiple redundant facilities for launching and landing aircraft and ships and multiple methods of moving personnel and equipment on land, sea, air, and in orbit.

2.2. Multiple Redundancies

For all capabilities related to the Three Pillars of Independence, the country must have multiple backups and interconnections among the decentralized functional elements that can be activated, deactivated, and reconfigured in real time as events unfold. The general rule is that all nodes in a decentralized distribution network for food, energy, or defense assets must be connected to adjacent nodes by more than one alternative pathway, except nodes that are at the end of a distribution path, and that such alternative pathways can be engaged in less time than it takes for the outage to cause negative effects for the consumers or recipients.

For example, given an electricity-generating facility that supplies consumers in a particular region of the country, should the facility be taken offline abruptly by an unforeseen event (earthquake, flood, terrorist attack, equipment failure, etc.), the system must be designed so that electricity generated elsewhere in the network can be routed to the consumers normally served by the disabled facility. The switch must occur in less time than it takes for a consumer's local backup system (diesel-powered generator, battery system, etc.) to be exhausted It is reasonable to expect some kinds of consumers to have backup facilities (hospitals, government buildings, etc.). Private homes may not be equipped with backup electrical facilities, so the rerouting of electrical transmission must take place in less time than it would take for people's food supplies to be affected by the outage.

2.3. Standards

When economies of scale are achieved through the strategy of building many small copies of a resource and networking them together, rather than the strategy of building a few large resources, then it becomes important for functional elements of the system to be based on standard parts and interfaces. For example, in the present-day US, it is not feasible to remove control rods from a reactor in Plant A and insert them into a reactor in Plant B to deal with an emergency situation, because the parts are not interchangeable. The two plants might use some of the same parts, such as pressure relief values, but those parts are not installed in a way that facilitates rapid removal and replacement, or that allows removal while the plant remains operational. Standards are needed that allow nearly any part to be replaced with only minimal interruptions to operations.

A second consideration, mainly pertaining to the Defense Pillar, is the standardization of training and skill sets for the maintenance and operation of electrical generating facilities. If a power plant is attacked by a foreign enemy or terrorist group, the personnel working there may be killed. Personnel who work at other, similar facilities around the country must have the knowledge and skills to repair the damaged facility and bring it back online.

2.4. Infrastructure

For purposes of this backgrounder, infrastructure means the framework of connections among decentralized facilities. It includes any kind of interconnections that are relevant to the purpose and function of one or more of the three pillars of independence, such as transportation, communication, and information redundancy and sharing.

2.5. Sustainability

For purposes of this backgrounder, a sustainable solution is one that is designed to function for a long time without catastrophic breakdown, allows for ongoing maintenance and repair, is designed to be technically updated or extended, and is designed to enable functional improvement without requiring a full replacement of the system.

2.6. Resiliency

For purposes of this backgrounder, a resilient solution is one that can absorb the impact of multiple technical failures, natural disasters, or intentional human sabotage or direct attacks at multiple sites and with multiple methods, while maintaining at least 50% of its normal service level to users, and that lends itself to rapid repair and restoration of full service at reasonable cost.

2.7. Education

Nothing can ensure the functionality and robustness of the three pillars more effectively than a population that is well-informed about the value and importance of the interconnected systems, and their stake in the system's success. Elements of the State educational core curriculum will provide such education to young people, and an ongoing public outreach program will offer information and updates to the entire population.

2.8. Consistent Enforcement

Almost every physical component of the systems that support the three pillars of independence will be designed, built, and operated by private sector companies on a for-profit basis. Private-sector companies are much better able to design and build capable and robust systems than any government agency could do.

The profit motive is a wonderful incentive, but there is a downside: Excessive greed can lead to short-term thinking. Long-term thinking is an absolute requirement of the three pillars. Therefore, business activities that pertain to the three pillars must be regulated to ensure the requirements for resiliency and sustainability are met.

The present-day practice in the US to accept the lowest bid for all projects, or to trade government contracts for financial or political favors, will not serve the needs of the people under the State system. The creativity and innovation of the private sector must be turned toward discovering cost-effective ways to meet the need, rather than clever ways to cut costs.

3. The Food Pillar

Independence in food production, quality, and distribution does not mean that every food product Americans are accustomed to consuming must be produced domestically (although that is possible in future, in principle). The imported foods we enjoy are luxury items, not necessities. It may be hard for Americans to imagine life without coffee, tea, chocolate, Asian spices, European beer and wine, those special foreign cheeses, or year-round supplies of fresh fruits and vegetables without regard to season. The truth is that none of these things is mandatory for survival; in fact, they are not necessary for us to enjoy a wide variety of tasty and healthy foods.

3.1. Goals

The basic goal of the food pillar is to create a sustainable system in which Americans' everyday lives are unaffected by any hostile action taken by a foreign power or a domestic terror/activist/revolutionary actor, up to and including nuclear war (short of an armageddon-style war), and any common natural phenomena such as floods, draught, tornadoes, earthquakes, hurricanes, volcanoes, and meteor strikes. If they can't hurt us, then they can't take away our freedom. Of course, Americans in the immediate target zone of a major natural event or attack would be affected; but the nation as a whole would still have its food supply.

Realistically, if America ever needed to manage entirely on domestic foods alone, that need would be temporary. It is far more likely that some food imports from some foreign trade partners would become unavailable during an international dispute. Eventually the dispute would be settled, or we would find alternative sources for our favorite imports, or a domestic industry would emerge to meet demand.

Even so, having the ability to live indefinitely - and well - without food imports puts America in a strong position. It means Americans' freedom cannot be compromised by any foreign actor putting pressure on our food supply.

3.2. Strengths

The contiguous North American territory of the present-day US, and therefore of the proposed American State, includes most of the world's most desirable arable land. The land is well protected from external access by two large mountain ranges, but there are no significant barriers to movement of foods within the country.

America is a leading producer of wheat, rice, and corn - the main staples of the human diet. The quality of beef raised in America is the best in the world - a status shared by some of our neighbors in the Western Hemisphere. (Japanese Kobe beef is not a comparable product, as it is not accessible to most people due to its cost.) The other primary meats in Americans' diet, pork and chicken, are raised in abundance domestically. We also have other animals in large numbers that could be used for meat, if it became necessary - notably turkeys, ostriches, emus, rabbits, sheep, goats, and horses. Most Americans are unaccustomed to eating these animals, but that is a matter of habit and custom, not necessity.

The climate zones and soil in different parts of America lend themselves to different types of crops, and for raising different kinds of food animals. Long coastlines along the Atlantic and Pacific oceans and the Gulf of Mexico, as well as numerous large lakes and rivers, are sources of a wide range of fish and other water-dwelling animals, both wild and farmed.

Many varieties of fruits and nuts grow natively in temperate latitudes, and America raises most of them. A few tropical fruits can be grown in the southern US on a seasonal basis. By taking better advantage of leading-edge agricultural technologies, including vertical farming, hydroponic and aeroponic growing methods, intensive water use reduction and water recycling, and genetic engineering, we can begin to provide a domestic supply of literally any and all plant-based foods - including our favorite imports, like coffee and chocolate.

The regional river system is unparalleled anywhere else on the planet, and in modern times transportation infrastructure includes rail, highway, and air as well as river transport.

3.3. Vulnerabilities

To achieve economies of scale - and, unfortunately, to concentrate more wealth in the hands of the power elite - agriculture in the US has steadily become more and more centralized. Fewer and fewer companies (and the elite families behind them) control more and more of the food supply. The history behind this change is a disturbing account of the rich and powerful squeezing small farmers off the land and impoverishing them in order to increase profits only marginally. This is a glaring example of greed run amok.

Occasionally we are reminded of the risks of centralization. During the Coronavirus pandemic of 2020-2021, reductions in output from just a couple of processing facilities had a severe impact on the supply of chicken to consumers. Fortunately, the shortage was temporary. Similarly, in the previous century, the epidemic of "mad cow disease" impacted America's supply of beef.

A decentralized system could have isolated these problems to one or two facilities, while leaving many more facilities in full operation. That would require a higher initial investment by the owners. The financial incentives at the time did not encourage them to do this. We can provide different incentives, and achieve better results.

3.4. Possible Strategies

Under vulnerabilities, we noted that US agricultural companies have sought economies of scale by centralizing operations in a few very large facilities. Another, more sustainable way to achieve economies of scale is by decentralizing operations into a large number of much smaller facilities, distributed over a large geographical area. This approach results in a system that is more sustainable, resilient, and robust than a system that can be crippled by disabling a single facility, or a handful of facilities.

By taking advantage of modern technologies, most of these distributed facilities should, in principle, be able to produce almost any plant-based foodstuffs. We have options today that did not exist just a few years ago - indoor, underground, underwater, floating farms, and orbital food production facilities are all well-protected from variations in seasonal weather patterns, occasional extreme weather events, and long-term climate change. The decentralized approach also offers resiliency in the face of natural geological events, biological issues within a facility (a plant or animal disease, for instance) and human-instigated disruptions, as the workload of a disabled facility can be picked up by the rest of the network until the disabled facility is back online.

3.5. Implications for International Trade

The shift from imperialism based on military force to international influence based on economic power means the State will use the advantages of America to improve our relationships with other countries. Philosophically, we intend this in a positive way. For Americans to be safe, prosperous, and free, our neighbors must also be safe, prosperous, and free. We can help them, and in so doing, help ourselves, as well.

We are convinced that this will be a more effective approach than forcing others to bend to our will at gunpoint. We believe it will be both easier and more cost-effective for us, too.

But the real world is not all unicorns and rainbows. Every other government in the world is not interested in stability, prosperity, and peace. Some are interested in hegemony and global cultural dominance. Others may be unable to free themselves from their historical hatred of the old United States. Our advantages with respect to food production place us in a favorable position with respect to those actors, too.

Some analysts believe America could, in theory, feed itself and the rest of the world comfortably. If that is even partially true, then the situation provides immense leverage in international affairs. We need not impose sanctions or establish embargoes against anyone. All we need to do is provide them foodstuffs on favorable terms; an offer they can't refuse, so to speak. In any case, most of them can't afford to refuse.

This strategy supports the defense pillar, as well. Would any foreign country destroy its own food supply? It is possible some foreign governments would be willing to do so, to prove the point that they hate America. But even for those governments, it would be perhaps the single stupidest decision they could possibly make.

4. The Energy Pillar

An aspect of sustainable independence is the ability of a country to provide for its own energy needs without dependencies on foreign supplies.

4.1. Goals

The general goal of the energy pillar is to ensure that power continues to flow for all domestic users even when one or more nodes in the energy network is affected by natural or human-generated outages. Of course, the people living at or near the sites struck by a natural event, technical problem, or attack will necessarily be affected, but ideally the system as a whole continues to operate, with power routed around any problem spots, until all elements of the system have been restored to full operation. In addition to resiliency against natural events, technical issues, and attacks, we want to support routine maintenance and upgrades with minimal impact to users.

4.2. Strengths

According to the United States Energy Information Administration, as of 2021 the US produced over 4,000 billion kilowatthours of electricity from the following sources:

  • natural gas (38.3%)
  • coal (21.8%)
  • nuclear (18.9%)
  • renewables (20.1%)
    • non-hydroelectric renewables (13.8%)
      • wind (11%)
      • solar (2.8%)
    • hydroelectric renewables (6.3%)
  • petroleum and other (0.9%)

The same source reports a sharp upward trend in the use of wind and solar electricity generation.

In 2017, the US became a net exporter of natural gas, and has remained a net exporter until this writing (2022). Yet, the US imports natural gas, too. Almost all natural gas imports are from Canada, and nearly all of that comes by pipeline.

As of the start of 2021, the demonstrated reserve base (DRB) of coal was 472 short tons. This resource represents more potential energy generation than natural gas and petroleum reserves.

The US has been a net exporter of petroleum and petroleum products since 2020. Imports peaked in 2007. The US gradually shifted its sources for petroleum imports from OPEC countries to Canada. The US exports petroleum and petroleum liquids (mostly propane) to India, Mexico, South Korea, Canada, Netherlands, and China.

Sources of uranium for nuclear power plants came from these sources, as of 2021:

  • Kazakhstan (35%)
  • Canada (15%)
  • Australia (14%)
  • Russia (14%)
  • Namibia (7%)
  • United States - domestic (5%)
  • other (10%)

The information above pertains to industrial-scale power generation - the "grid." There is also a growing movement for homeowners to install solar panels on their houses. Usually, these are connected to the grid and pull power from the grid when the home's consumption exceeds the capacity of the solar panels on the house. In most locales, homeowners also sell their excess electricity back into the grid. In some cases, the homeowners install a battery system to store energy, and they can operate when the grid is down.

Both the range of different types of electricity generation and the trends toward more-sustainable options are strengths for building the energy pillar.

4.3. Vulnerabilities

The vulnerabilities in the present-day US power system are due to short-cuts taken in the interest of short-term and relatively small profits over long-term, substantial and sustainable profits. The root cause of nearly all the problems in the energy sector is simply the old question of delayed gratification; no more complicated than the well-known Marshmallow Experiment.

That experiment has been reevaluated and analyzed from various perspectives; in the present context, we choose to focus on the fact the test involved children. We assert that making choices in business that favor long-term, sustainable benefit is not difficult - it is, literally, child's play. Yet, American business leaders have demonstrated over many years that they are incapable of making, or unwilling to make those choices. They always eat the first marshmallow immediately. Far from being heroes of industry and noteworthy economic leaders, they are children who have no self-control.

The companies responsible for designing, building, and maintaining the electrical grid have taken the cheapest possible approach to everything they have done, with a very short-term view. They would rather earn one dollar today, in a way that causes the system to fail next week, than to invest properly to set things up so that they can earn ten dollars next month, and every month thereafter. The problem manifests on multiple levels:

  • the pursuit of economies of scale by the strategy of building relatively few large, centralized electrical generating plants;
  • the complexity of the connections, which grew over a long period of time;
  • delayed and careless maintenance and repairs of electrical equipment; and
  • the repeated pattern of creating hard dependencies on foreign imports when such dependencies were unnecessary.

It's true that economies of scale can be achieved by building one or a few very high-capacity electricity-generating facilities; but they can also be achieved by building a large number of much smaller facilities, networking them in a way that provides resiliency and flexibility. The initial costs may be higher, but the long-term benefits are much greater.

The resulting system is more flexible for routing power to wherever it is needed and for adjusting to localized peak demands; more resilient when equipment failures occur or when damage is done to some nodes; more cost-effective to maintain due to common designs and interchangeable components; safer due to the limited scope of events that affect small facilities on a local level. Overall quality of service is higher, as an outage in a small facility will affect far fewer people than an outage that affects an entire region of the country.

In the nuclear energy sector, safety plays into this consideration, as well. Large reactors can develop unstoppable and deadly problems when things go wrong. Certain place names are remembered solely for such disasters, and are not known for anything else. Who today can tell us anything interesting about Three Mile Island, Chernobyl, or Fukushima that is unrelated to nuclear reactor failures? All these were large-scale facilities running multiple large reactors. A network of smaller reactors provides better safety, as a catastrophic failure of a small reactor can be contained and managed.

We attribute the choices made in the past to short-term greed. The State will encourage companies to consider long-term benefit above immediate profit. If they can but wait for the second marshmallow, their profits will be greater. Isn't that what they want - greater profit? If business leaders cannot see this, then the State will be a guide dog to lead them.

The complexity of a system can be necessary or arbitrary. The complexity of the present power network is, in part, necessary - it is a complex job on the merits. But for the most part, the complexity of the system is arbitrary, resulting from a lack of planning for ongoing growth, upgrades, and maintenance.

In every case, new equipment is tacked onto the existing network in the cheapest way possible, with no thought to maintenance, longevity, resiliency, or future expansion. We attribute this to a combination of short-term greed in the private sector and Byzantine, overlapping, contradictory rules in the public sector owing to the large number of regulatory authorities in play - one of the many problems caused by having 50 separate states.

The deterioration and failures of the grid over the years due to delays in maintenance, and in some cases failure to maintain the equipment at all, is another manifestation of short-term thinking and greed. Over time, it is less costly to keep the equipment in good working order than it is to cross our fingers and hope for the best in order to avoid maintenance costs altogether.

In the post World War II period, the US established economic hegemony over the world by opening up free trade and defending global shipping lanes with the US Navy. Over time, this has had both positive and negative effects for the US and for the world. One reality during this period is that the US really has not absolutely required any imports of raw materials for energy production, but purchased imports anyway to stimulate global trade.

Unfortunately, American companies and consumers came to depend on these imports, and when events occurred that made it harder to get such imports at low cost (like the formation of OPEC in the 1970s), the national economy was unable to adjust quickly. This happened because of short-term greed - the cheapest thing American companies could possibly do was to sit back and allow the imports to come flooding in; they didn't bother with back-up plans, and weren't prepared to pivot away from the imports when the time came to do so.

The State will use economic generosity as a tool of foreign policy as well, but will take care not to create unnecessary dependencies when in fact an import agreement is meant to serve a strategic purpose. America has the resources to take care of itself; we don't need hard dependencies on foreign sources. Our import-export strategy is just that - a strategy.

4.3.1. Electrical Grid

The electrical grid is fragile, and Americans have enjoyed amazing good luck. The occasional large-scale outage demonstrates how little it takes to deny power to large swathes of the US.

In 1965, 30 million people in the northeastern US were without power for 13 hours. The cause was sloppy maintenance work. Someone set protective relays incorrectly, and a small power surge tripped the relay and deactivated the transmission line. The event is popularly called the Northeast Blackout of 1965.

In 1977, New York City suffered a blackout due to a single lightning strike on a single substation. Thanks to sloppy maintenance work (loose locking nuts), the power remained off long enough for two subsequent lightning strikes to take down the largest generator for the city.

In 1979, equipment failures at the nuclear power plant at Three Mile Island, Pennsylvania, caused a meltdown of one of the facility's nuclear reactor cores. The main equipment failure was a stuck relief valve, which prevented heat from being removed from one of the cores. Subsequent actions by plant operators exacerbated the problem, before they realized there was a problem with the valve. Fortunately, there were no lasting adverse effects, although about 2 million people were exposed to some level of radiation.

In late 1982, high winds along the west coast of the US knocked over a transmission tower, which crashed into a line tower, in turn causing three more towers to fall. Backup plans failed because they were not designed to handle an outage of such scope. Communication problems exacerbated the problem and delayed repairs.

High demand during the hot summer of 1996 overloaded the grid and caused two massive power outages affecting Idaho, Utah, and California. About two million people were affected. Fortunately, service was restored within a couple of hours.

A powerful lightning storm in Minnesota in 1998 took out a power line, causing a transmission failure. A second lightning strike broke the connection between transmission lines. The northern Midwest was separated from the Eastern grid, and people in the upper Midwest and central Canada were without power for 19 hours.

In 2019, the Camp Fire in California was caused by faulty power lines installed through forested land, at two ignition points. Dry vegetation, high winds, high temperatures, and low humidity, sped the spread of the fire once it started. The fire killed 85 people, destroyed 153,000 acres of forest, and destroyed 19,000 houses and other buildings.

In 2021, during an uncharacteristically severe winter, the Texas power grid experienced major outages. The event is considered the worst energy infrastructure failure in Texas history. Between 246 (confirmed) and 702 (estimated) people died. Over 4.5 million homes and businesses were without electricity, and there were shortages of water, food, and heat. Total financial damage exceeded $195 billion. All the problems were caused by inadequate maintenance and contingency planning - failure to winterize equipment exposed to the weather. The maintenance failures were caused, in turn, by greed and short-term thinking.

It is remarkable that more outages and other incidents related to the electrical grid have not occurred over the years. The system has not been significantly upgraded, protected, or made redundant based on these experiences. Power companies and regulatory agencies continue to operate as if nothing had ever gone wrong and there were no lessons to be learned.

The common causes of all these events are:

  • power lines are installed above ground on poles and towers, instead of below ground within conduits designed and built for resiliency and ease of maintenance - more costly initially, but less risky and less costly to maintain;
  • careless maintenance - setting relays incorrectly, leaving locking bolts unsecured, and allowing equipment installed in remote locations to deteriorate without inspection or repair; and
  • the generally haphazard growth of the grid, which is really a number of separate grids that are interconnected in ad hoc ways, and that include a great deal of very old equipment.

The condition of the electrical grid(s) in the US poses a direct risk not only to the delivery of power to users, but also to the food and defense pillars. It would be easy to cripple the US by attacking a handful of well-selected targets. Assuming the State system is implemented, correcting this will be among the first priorities of the State.

4.3.2. Fuel for Industry and Transportation

According to the US Energy Information Administration as of 26 July, 2022, US domestic field production of crude oil stands at approximately 12 million barrels per day, subject to some variation, while the country imports about 6.8 million barrels per day, with OPEC members accounting for roughly one tenth of the imports. At the same time, the US exports about 3.2 million barrels per day.

Our rough, and probably naive analysis is that as of the time of writing the US produces nearly twice as much crude oil as it imports, and is not strongly dependent on OPEC countries for the imports. Exports amount to about half of the imported quantity. If we produce 12 million barrels per day, import another 6.8 million, and export 3.2 million, then our domestic demand is about 15.6 million barrels per day.

The US appears able to use petroleum import and export levels to direct the global economy to some extent, although there are other players who can push their own petroleum policies without the approval of the US. Of the imports, about 3.5 million barrels per day come from Mexico, a friendly country; but about 1.6 million barrels per day come from Russia, a country openly hostile to the US. This suggests there is more to the story than straightforward business transactions. Meanwhile, the US imports no petroleum from Venezuela, a country from which we bought significant quantities of crude before the Chavez regime overturned democracy there. This is another indication that the US is successfully using petroleum to support foreign policy.

According to the same source, Texas field production amounts to slightly more than 5 million barrels per day, a significant proportion of total US production. As the State intends to separate Texas from America, this poses a risk to the energy pillar. While the State plans to treat the new nation of Texas as a Category 2 country (principal trade partner) as well as Category 3 (immediate neighbor with a land border) and Category 4 (resident of our "neighborhood" - the Americas), there is no guarantee that Texas would consider the new America to be a friendly country or a desirable business partner. Even if they do so, they may decide other customers are more lucrative for their petroleum exports.

Therefore, America must be prepared to meet domestic demand, including growth until such time as alternative fuels become mainstream. Of course, all the numbers will change with the absence of Texas. Part of US demand comes from there, and some US imports go to support Texas. To compute the demand in the American State sans Texas, we used the figures from Statista for Texas consumption of 6,185.8 trillion British Thermal Units for petroleum, and 4,708.4 BTU for natural gas. 4.4.1. Assessment, Upgrade, and Strengthening of the Electrical Grid

That means multiple, redundant, and fail-safe connections to enable re-routing of power as needed when outages occur. If routine and predictable causes like lightning strikes, high winds, cold weather, or the Aurora Borealis literally fry the equipment, then the system is not resilient. Some aspects of electrical grid upgrading and some design considerations for new equipment are based on the vulnerability of the system to electromagnetic pulse (EMP) effects. 4.4.2. Expansion of Solar Generation Capacity at Point of Use

In the present-day US, more and more homeowners are purchasing solar energy systems for their houses. The State will provide economic incentives to encourage this trend. For Americans who are not in a financial position to purchase solar energy systems, the State will offer subsidies. Neighborhood systems can be rolled out faster than systems installed on individual homes, and access for maintenance and upgrades will be easier. 4.4.3. Expansion of Solar and Wind Capacity Based on a Decentralized Model

4.4.4. Exploration of Alternative Battery Technologies and Battery Production Methods

It is desirable to move away from fossil fuels for energy production, but there is no such thing as a magic bullet. With the increased use of batteries comes increased environmental impact from their production. Current lithium-ion battery technology depends on mining and processing lithium. Present methods of mining and processing lithium have severe environmental impact. By increasing demand for lithium-ion batteries, we will simultaneously increase this environmental impact.

According to the Institute for Energy Research, the process to extract lithium ore uses "approximately 500,000 gallons [of water] per metric ton of lithium. To extract lithium, miners drill a hole in salt flats and pump salty, mineral-rich brine to the surface. After several months the water evaporates, leaving a mixture of manganese, potassium, borax, and lithium salts which is then filtered and placed into another evaporative pool. After between 12 and 18 months of this process, the mixture is filtered sufficiently that lithium carbonate can be extracted."

Lithium processing involves caustic and poisonous chemicals, including hydrochloric acid, and it is common for these chemicals to leak from the processing facilities into the local watter supply. China, Chile, Argentina, Bolivia, Tibet, Australia, and the United States have suffered environmental damage from lithium production. Damage affects the soil, air, and water. Dead animals have been found floating downriver from production sites; in Nevada, as far as 150 miles away from the production site.

A related problem is the disposal and/or recycling of lithium waste from spent batteries and discarded devices. Some of this waste could be used to produce new batteries, thus reducing demand for fresh mining and processing operations. Other forms of disposal and recycling could, at least, reduce the overall environmental impact of the industry. The Institute for Energy Research estimates that in China alone, recycling lithium to produce batteries (less efficient than new ones, but still functional), "for China's 5G stations or...shared e-bikes" could save 63 million tons of carbon emissions compared with manufacturing those batteries new.

The State will promote, and if necessary directly fund and direct, research into improved methods of lithium production, improved processes for the disposal and recycling of lithium, and new types of batteries that do not depend on dangerous chemicals in the first place. There are no guarantees of when or to what extent this research will bear fruit.

We are encouraged by some contemporary developments in this area. An Australian company started by two University of Melbourne students, Relectrify, has developed a way to repurpose lithium-ion batteries from vehicles to power home electrical systems. It takes much more energy to move a vehicle than it does to power the lights and appliances in a home. When lithium-ion batteries are no longer capable of powering a car, they still contain as much as 80% of their energy potential. Relectrify can repurpose these batteries for use in home electrical systems.

A technical improvement that may be useful as an interim solution is the lithium-sulfur battery. At the time of writing, people are working to overcome some of the technical challenges with this technology - in particular, the rapid degradation of the electrodes - but the technology may offer 40% higher energy density and lower production costs than lithium-ion batteries.

An improvement such as lithium-sulfur technology would reduce the environmental impact of battery production and disposal, but not eliminate the problem of lithium mining and processing. There is a technology "arms race" in progress, with companies and universities experimenting with alternatives to lithium-ion batteries. People involved in these efforts always say they are on the verge of a breakthrough. Maybe some of them are right.

But most of these ideas do not get us away from caustic chemicals. For instance the redox flow battery is a dual-liquid battery that uses hydrochloric acid and sulfuric acid. Stanford University has been experimenting with aluminum-graphite battery technology; this is at an early stage of research. MIT and Samsung are working on solid-state battery technology. These options still depend on basic materials that are currently used in commercially-available batteries or that are equally dangerous.

More-exotic directions of research include graphene superconductors, which are not exactly batteries at all. Theoretically, these can charge and discharge more efficiently than a battery and last much longer, although they do not hold as much energy per unit of volume as a battery. To bring this technology to market will require significant advances in nanotechnology. Even more exotic, and at an even more preliminary stage of development, is the bioelectrochemical battery. This technology, being investigated in the Netherlands, uses anaerobic bacteria to process acetate that releases electrons through reduction/oxidation.

While the alternatives to lithium-ion technology are not commercially viable at this time, the activity in this space tells us that people are creative and clever about developing new energy technology, and one or more of their ideas will ultimately become viable. We need to design our future energy solutions to be upgradeable as new solutions emerge.

4.4.5. "Clean Coal" as a Backup Energy Source

As noted above under strengths, US reserves of coal exceed the energy potential of petroleum and gas reserves. Coal still in the ground is a tremendous resource, too.

Clearly, we do not want to use coal as our primary energy source. There are significant health and safety issues in the mining and processing of coal, and severe negative environmental impact when it is burned. However, we cannot ignore the coal reserves as a backup resource in case international tensions force America to fend for itself temporarily on occasion.

To that end, the State will encourage two developments: First, a reasonable number of coal-powered electrical generating facilities will keep their coal equipment in place, maintain it, and periodically fire it up to ensure it is usable and that the nation can pivot to coal quickly, without interrupting service to customers. Second, those plants will be fitted with so-called "clean coal" technology, so that when we do resort to burning coal we are doing it in the most environmentally-responsible way possible.

4.4.8. Interim Strategy for Nuclear Energy

Continued use of nuclear power generation makes sense as an interim measure during the transition to more-sustainable energy production methods. Longer term, the strategy is to phase out nuclear energy as more alternative methods come online.

There are two factors that rule out nuclear energy as a long-term strategy. The first is the problem of disposing of the spent nuclear fuel. The second is the question of sourcing uranium, a non-renewable resource for which we cannot meet demand from domestic sources alone.

Safety concerns are largely solved by building small reactors rather than large ones. The technology as such is quite reliable. It is the scale of nuclear "incidents" that raises safety concerns. 4.4.9. Exploration of Alternative Vehicle Propulsion Technologies

In the present-day US, nearly all vehicles (except experimental ones) are powered by gasoline, diesel, or rechargeable batteries; some city buses in urban centers use liquified natural gas (LNG). But these are not the only technologies in use for vehicles around the world. Other choices include biodiesel, methane, LNG, and hydrogen fuel cell (electric). In some countries, multiple fuel technologies are in use concurrently as the country gradually phases out high-emission vehicles. When you visit a "gas station" or "petrol station" in one of those countries, you find different pumps for different fuels.

There have been other solutions in the past, including steam and turbine engines. Those two have certain inconveniences that make them impractical at scale, but their existence demonstrates that people can come up with innovative solutions to problems; we may see practical ideas for alternative propulsion in future, if we provide incentives for people to explore them.

We recall a story of "hillbilly engineering" years ago, when a man designed and built a wood-burning "reactor" to produce hydrogen to run his pickup truck (not a hydrogen fuel cell solution). Developed further, the technology is called a wood gasifier (see article in Mother Earth News). Innovative thinking like this will lead to solutions we cannot predict.

The main practical factor that has made it difficult for the US to adopt alternative vehicle propulsion systems is the physical size of the country. For a technology like, say, hydrogen fuel cells to become widely used in the US, hydrogen fuel stations would have to be set up on all Interstate highways and other major roadways, as well as throughout every city and town. As of 2020, the Interstate system alone had 48,756 miles (78,465 km) of roadways. To phase in technologies that can be fully supported by domestic sources, and that are more environmentally-friendly than current options, would require many years of carefully-managed change, and would involve supporting multiple technologies simulaneously in many thousands of fuelling stations.

An idea that has been tried before on a limited basis is the powered roadway. Rather than having each vehicle propel itself, the vehicles draw power from the roadway. To retrofit the Interstate highway system in this way (not to mention general roads) would be cost-prohibitive, and may never serve more than a small percentage of vehicles that use the system. Besides, a vehicle parked at someone's house would need a way to move itself to the nearest powered roadway, so this approach may add problems rather than solving them. However, powered roadways could be a good solution for local traffic within urban areas, especially types of vehicles that must be in motion most of the time and that tend to travel prescribed routes repeatedly, such as buses and delivery vans.

4.4.11. Assessment of Costs and Benefits of Expanded Rail Capacity

The US was among the first countries to build a nationwide rail network. To this day, rail transport is one of the most important ways goods make their way across the country. 5.2. Strengths

If implemented, the American State would inherit two primary strengths from the US in regard to the Defense Pillar. The first is the geography of the country, as described previously. The second is the world economic order that the US established in the wake of World War II.

5.2.1. The Bretton Woods System

Peter Zeihan summarizes the Bretton Woods Agreement well in his 2014 book, The Accidental Superpower. Our take in a nutshell, plus a couple of other salient points:

  • The US emerged from the war as the only global power; the USSR was a second near-global power, but lacked some of the advantages the US enjoyed; in fact, the USSR benefited from US assistance in rebuilding after the war. No other country was in a position to bootstrap itself after the war.
  • US military power was based on its navy; in the late 1940s, it was the only global naval force. Former maritime powers like Japan and Great Britain had lost most of their fleets. The US formula for military success was rapid deployment anywhere, rapid and decisive defeat of enemy forces, and timely withdrawal, before it became necessary to "manage" or "rule" the defeated country.
  • The US mainland was largely untouched by the war; industrial capacity was still intact, cities were still standing, and American forces had suffered fewer casualties than other participants in the war (on either side).
  • The US ended up in possession of a number of small territories in the Pacific and Caribbean that were critical staging points for naval operations and global trade.
  • Unlike previous conquering powers, the US was not capable of supporting long-term occupation forces to subdue conquered populations. The US lacked the administrative organization and the inclination to do this. In the cases when the US leadership forgot this, and kept US forces on the ground too long, the results were disastrous.
  • Further colonialist expansion was not possible, as there was no place left to expand into. Economic growth had to be based on international trade instead of imperialistic expansion into formerly-closed markets. All markets were now open, anyway.

Based on its own strengths and weaknesses, the economically-dominant US crafted a new financial world order. They called a summit meeting at Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, in 1944 that included 730 delegates of 44 nations. At that time, the US could have dictated anything to the rest of the world. No one was in a strong enough position to oppose them. Many of the delegates were surprised at the US proposal.

They opened the US market to international trade on terms favorable to everyone else. They basically agreed to purchase everything the other countries could produce, without making any particular demands. They offered financial assistance to rebuild destroyed countries. They established the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD), which later became the World Bank. They established a fixed currency exchange rate based on the gold standard (that bit did not last long, but created stability for a few years, which was sorely needed).

Zeihan has a very positive view of the Bretton Woods Agreement. The Corporate Finance Institute continues the story, including the collapse of the gold standard in the 1960s-70s. Even so, some of the more-successful modern nations built their economies on this system, and to this day they are fully dependent on the structure of international alliances that arose from it. Even some of the US opponents in the war, notably Japan and Germany, were able to build strong economies mainly because they did not have to provide for their own defense; they were able to invest funds that would normally go toward defense into economic development.

Instead of occupation forces that would be opposed by the people, US military presence in many other countries was welcomed. The US was helping them rebuild. It was not for altruistic reasons, but the assistance was quite real.

At any rate, the system that resulted from Bretton Woods became the basis for the US to become the single dominant economic force in the world. The system of alliances that emerged from World War II has been gradually deteriorating for some time now. The US strategy of quick interventions in other countries to enforce US economic hegemony is not working well anymore. From time to time, the US has engaged in the sort of long-term occupation for which it is ill-suited - Korea, Vietnam, Afghanistan, Iraq - at great cost. We call these misadventures "big mistakes." Other actors have found ways around the Bretton Woods system, such as OPEC and BRIC alliances. Changes are needed, if America is to maintain its dominant global economic position.

The State's approach to foreign policy takes advantage of the dominant position of the US and shifts international relations into a mode we believe is more appropriate for the coming century. We would not be in as strong a position today had history taken a different course. The American State would build on that foundation, even while using different policies and approaches to maintain its influence and sovereignty. We do not propose to give up that influence.

The effects of this component are similar to those of lightning. This is followed by a lower-energy and longer-duration pulse that flows through electrical transmission lines and damages the power grid, sometimes even fusing transmission lines so they no longer function, thus complicating repairs. This component also damages any equipment connected to the transmission lines.

Apart from natural causes, EMP is one of the effects of a thermonuclear detonation. One tactic an attacker may use against America is to detonate one or two nuclear warheads at a certain altitude above the target country. Any unprotected electronic circuits exposed to the effect would become non-functional.

A nuclear warhead is a pretty expensive way to generate an EMP, and has obvious downsides due to its general destructive force and residual radiation. If a potentially-hostile country becomes dependent on us economically, especially for basic needs such as food, this will reduce the likelihood they will choose to attack us. If we build cultural connections with another country, they will be less interested in harming us.

According to our categorization of foreign countries, a country openly Hostile towards America can become an existential threats to America, or can move in the other direction and become a neutral country with an Unfavorable View of America. Our strategy for international relations would be to encourage hostile countries to become at least neutral, even if they maintain an unfavorable view of America. This is a better outcome for both America and the other country.

The second layer, still pertaining to prevention, is intelligence. If we can anticipate a potential enemy's decision to launch an attack, we can take action before the attack is launched to disable (or at least, reduce) their capability. In the Cold War era, people used the term "pre-emptive first strike" to describe this tactic.

"Pre-emptive first strike" does not mean an all-out nuclear attack against the other country. The term can also refer to a pre-emptive attack against specific targets, with the goal of reducing the enemy's capability to launch a crippling attack against America.

For example, should active hostilities arise between America and Russia we could have a pre-planned operation ready to go to eliminate all five of their Borei-class nuclear missile submarines simultaneously. Our military tracks the locations of all Russian ships, and we know where to find the targets. That is only an example; of course, that action would be coordinated with other operations to reduce their offensive capability before they initiated their general attack. Unfortunately, there is no practical way to eliminate their offensive capability altogether.

We have an advantage with respect to Russia in that each class of submarine in their fleet is serviced by a single port. Eliminating one port will reduce the effectiveness of that entire class of ships; not instantaneously, but the ships would steadily lose effectiveness throughout the war, as they could not be repaired or resupplied except at sea. Of course, these preemptive strikes would not entirely eliminate their ability to strike American territory, but they would reduce the potential damage from such an attack.

We use Russia as the example because it is the most potent potential enemy country as of the time of writing, and not because we are looking for trouble with Russia. We are not looking for trouble with anyone. We just want to be prepared for all contingencies. If we can deal with Russia, then we can deal with anyone else. In a sense, they are the benchmark for this sort of planning.

5.4.5.2. Delay

When prevention is not possible, the next word in the list is forestall, meaning delay or postpone. This is essentially what the US did throughout the Cold War in the 20th century. The primary ways the US accomplished that were (a) economic aid and various discreet deals with the USSR, mostly not publicized, or described by both sides in flamboyantly-patriotic and factually-inaccurate terms; and (b) ensuring the Kremlin understood what would happen to Russia if they initiated a nuclear war with the West. The State would continue with these tactics.

5.4.5.3. Risk Reduction and Damage Mitigation - Decentralization and Redundancy

The idea of reducing the risk or mitigating the effects of an attack represents the third layer in the defensive strategy. The phrase "reduce the risk of" means to minimize the extent of the damage to a particular target. The phrase "mitigate the effects of" means to ensure the system as a whole continues to operate even when some of its components (i.e., individual targets) have been destroyed or damaged.

To achieve these goals, we need resiliency - the ability to continue operating even when heavily damaged. The primary way to improve resiliency of the overall "system" (the whole country) is decentralization. No single critical capability can be centralized in one or a few locations, and the connections between components of the system must not be organized in a way that presents one or a few targets whose destruction would disable the network.

To continue with Russia as the example (only for discussion purposes, of course), to destroy America physically they would have to destroy more than 40 large metropolitan areas and hundreds of defense-related sites. In contrast, to destroy Russia physically would require the destruction of perhaps 6 or 7 significant targets; it is realistic to expect after the loss of 3 or 4 primary targets, they would cease hostilities. While such a war would result in significant and tragic losses on our side, America would survive and rebuild; Russia would not. And there are no other countries in the world that pose a threat on the same scale as Russia.

In the section about the Energy Pillar, we alluded to the general way in which large cities have emerged, and mentioned the difficulty in planning cities. The challenge for defense of each urban area is the physical concentration of people and facilities within a small footprint. The challenge at national scale is the location of critical capabilities in a single city, or just a couple of cities.

Decentralization within the urban area can be accomplished (in principle) by spreading things around; changing from a central core with supporting infrastructure surrounding it, and residential development surrounding that in turn, into a series of smaller urban centers, each with its own self-contained facilities for business, industry, and living. On the larger scale, spreading critical capabilities across multiple cities separated by long distances improves decentralization and therefore resiliency. The country as a whole would still be operational following a large-scale nuclear attack. After our military response, the attacking country would probably not be operational. This is not something to wish for, but at least America would survive and could rebuild.

An example of a large urban area that is internally decentralized is the Dallas/Ft. Worth Metroplex in northern Texas. Although we are not thinking of Texas as part of the new America due to its political and cultural values, that metropolitan area offers an example of what we mean by decentralization. Both Dallas and Ft. Worth have their historical downtown cores, but the area comprises a number of small "downtowns" separated from each other by several miles (in many cases, more than 10 miles). The whole thing is spread over an area about 100 miles across from east to west and about 30 miles north to south.

Everything is redundantly connected with modern transportation and communication infrastructure. Most large businesses that operate there are spread out in multiple business parks that are well separated. The population is spread more-or-less evenly across the whole area. Apart from the two airports, there are few individual targets where an attack could eliminate a key industrial or operational capability. Even the airports have remote backup sites - a number of military and civilian airports lie within a 10 to 15 minute flight of Dallas.

Deep inside friendly territory, the only practical way for an enemy force to reach this metropolitan area is by air. Using conventional weapons such as bombers and long-range artillery, it would take massive amounts of ammunition and quite a long time to disable the crtiical capabilities housed in the target area. Defensive forces would be able to eliminate the attackers before they could succeed in this.

Using nuclear weapons, an enemy would need several warheads to take this asset completely out of commission, delivered with high precision and careful timing. By the time the inbound missiles were on final approach, military assets would have been distributed throughout the area and air defenses activated. A few missiles would penetrate; there is no magic, here. But the damage could be mitigated. It is not a realistic military objective, even for a nuclear power like Russia.

American cities have not been planned with decentralization in mind explicitly, and certainly not for the purpose of national defense - or national survivability. Yet, many American cities are spread out over large geographical areas as compared with European cities. Apart from having many targets to strike, an enemy would have to concentrate a good deal of force on each of those targets in order to eliminate them.

Some of the older cities in the eastern US are more vulnerable, as they are compact and centralized, and in some cases geography has not allowed for expansion. The strategy of decentralization can still mitigate damage in that region at the larger scale, even if certain individual cities could be lost.

5.4.5.4. Risk Reduction and Damage Mitigation - Resistance to EMP Attacks

Standards for civilian and military electronic devices and for electrical transmission and storage equipment will include provisions for protection against EMP effects. In addition to the fields of engineering already involved in the design of such equipment, new standards will call for electromagnetic compatibility (EMC) factors to be included in all new equipment, as well as retrofitted existing equipment, including civilian electrical and electronic systems and military assets.

The State will push for comprehensive upgrading of all existing equipment, beginning immediately. We anticipate this to be among the most expensive programs in the State's development of the Defense and Energy Pillars, because at the starting point there is practically nothing in place.

5.4.5.5. Establish Credible Threat of Punishment or Annihilation

The idea of deterrence was part of US defense strategy in the 20th century. In the Cold War, there was a concept of "Mutually Assured Destruction," or MAD, that stated if either the USSR or the US initiated a nuclear war, both countries would be destroyed.

The American State has a different philosophy regarding war. We will not tolerate any existential threats to America, as that would be contrary to the purpose of the State - ensuring sustainable independence and freedom for Americans. We have a "last resort" mentality regarding war - it will be the last (final) act of any agressor.

5.4.5. Strengthen Cyber Defense Capability

There are different sources of vulnerability to cyberattack, so the potential strategies must be multifaceted. The first line of defense is education. The State educational system includes general life skills and information about how to "navigate the system;" that is, how to carry out necessary tasks in life. In a high-tech world, understanding how to prevent information theft is a basic life skill. Knowledge about how to avoid risks of cyberattack on an individual level will be included in the core curriculum. Most companies and government agencies already provide periodic refreshers on this subject, and that will continue under the State system.

Another aspect of education is to educate the general public that all companies are susceptible to this kind of attack. Currently, many companies keep attacks secret for fear their reputation will be damaged and customers will avoid doing business with them. We need to eliminate this stigma from our culture. A cyberattack is no different from a mugging on the street; it is not the victim's fault, even if one could argue that the victim did not practice situational awareness.

Beyond the stigma and reputational damage, another problem with secrecy is that each company is on its own in opposition to hacking organizations that are far more capable and sophisticated. By sharing information and cooperating in corrective actions, companies gain the benefit of everyone else's experience and resources.

Establishing standards for cybersecurity is tricky, because the threat landscape is constantly changing. However, some basic things can be defined as a baseline for security. As mentioned under vulnerabilities in section 5.3.5, the use of passwords is a problem, and the way passwords are used makes the problem worse. We can at least establish a baseline standard that requires other methods of user authentication, known to be more effective than passwords, and we can require rational rules for password creation rather than special characters and numbers, etc.

Conceptually and practically, interfering in someone's life by stealing their identity or by stealing their account numbers prevents them from living their lives in freedom. Therefore, theft of personal information, publication of personal information ("doxxing"), and identity theft will be considered Category 1 offenses. Category 1 offenses can result in multi-decade prison terms. We hope this will serve as a deterrent, at least to some bad actors. History suggests most criminals do not pay attention to deterrents, but this can be one step among several to alleviate the problem.

As a hybrid authoritarian-democratic system, the State can cooperate with private-sector organizations more closely than under the present-day US system. When a company detects an intrusion into their systems, they can call on the government to assist them in setting traps in the system, tracing the origin of the intrusion, collecting evidence against the offenders, and prosecuting them. The government will have teams whose sole purpose is to combat cyberattacks, while most private companies will not. It makes sense to protect American business by providing this service gratis.

Legal remedies apply to domestic offenders only. Law enforcement's jurisdiction ends at America's borders. Therefore, when the perpetrators of information theft and other forms of cyberattacks are located in foreign countries, the problem becomes a matter of national defense. While we are aware this policy may set a dangerous precedent in international relations, we see no practical alternative than to define a cyberattack as equivalent to a kinetic attack. The State will assert the right to launch a physical response to cyberattacks originating outside America.

That said, when the attack originates in one of our primary trade partners, or a country in our "neighborhood," or a country with which America has an extradition agreement, the State will first attempt to engage law enforcement in the subject country, and cooperate with them to identify and arrest the perpetrators.

Another change in the legal system will help protect individual Americans from excessive damage due to identity theft. In the present-day US, once someone obtains the physical or electronic credentials that enable them to pose as someone else, the victim has little legal recourse and may face many years of expense and inconvenience to rectify the situation. Under the State system, Citizens and Residents will be in a national database. They can identify themselves biometrically, by DNA, fingrerprints, eye scans, and other means. They can demonstrate their true identity even if various "numbers" have been stolen. Law enforcement will tend to believe the victim when they report the crime, rather than assuming whoever is holding a driver's license or credit card is the actual person.

5.4.6. Guard Against Stochastic Terrorism

The State will prohibit the same activities as 18 USC 373, and will also prohibit stochastic terrorism. Rhetoric intended to raise doubts in people's minds about any group of people legally present in America will be a Category 1 offense, even if the rhetoric does not ask anyone in particular to carry out any specific act of violence against a named target.

There is a balancing act here - at what point does Constitutionally-protected free speech move from the realm of dissent into the realm of stochastic terrorism? To an extent, it may always be a judgment call. However, there is a key difference that may be used to distinguish between dissent and stochastic terrorism. When someone expresses dissent, they focus on the particular policies or actions with which they disagree. When we hear, instead, a constant refrain of "us versus them," and it seems that "they" are responsible for all the ills of the world, then where is the "dissent?" Clearly, the intent of such speech is only to divide people against each other.

A second indicator is the dehumanization of the "other." In the present-day US, supporters of the Republican Party frequently refer to supporters of the Democratic Party as "demon-rats," a play on "Democrats." Conversely, supporters of the Democratic Party refer to supporters of the Republican Party as "MAGAts" - a play on "maggots", referring to Donald Trump's campaign slogan, Make America Great Again (MAGA).

This is similar to Hutu references to Tutsi as "cockroaches" in the years leading up to the Rwandan genocide in the 1990s, and the Chavistas' references to the Venezuelan middle class as "escuálidos" in the ramp-up to the destruction of democracy in that country in the early years of the 21st century. We perceive the same pattern of rhetoric in the present-day US. It is not necessary to dehumanize anyone simply to express dissent with a policy or action. When dehumanization is part of the message, the message is not one of legitimate dissent.

We believe it will be possible to distinguish between the two accurately, and to protect Americans' freedom of speech while also defending the country against stochastic terrorism.

5.4.7. Enforce Laws Protecting the Three Pillars of Independence

While many aspects of the State are similar to corresponding aspects of the present-day US, some aspects are very different indeed. The most obvious difference is the change from 50 states to a single government. The second most significant difference may be the concept of the Three Pillars of Independence.

There are certain things that are absolutely mandatory if Americans are to enjoy individual liberty over a long period of time. Essentially, those are independence in food, energy, and defense. Under the State system, any action that undermines those Three Pillars of Independence will be treated as a Category 1 offense.

Maintaining the Three Pillars of Independence is considered a matter of national defense; a matter of the survivability of the country. For example, actions such as moving American jobs overseas where cheap labor may be found, at the cost of degrading the American economy and the quality of life of Americans, is a Category 1 offense because it compromises Americans' ability to live in freedom according to their own values and principles without external interference. No such concept exists in the present-day US. It may take some time for business leaders to work out how they need to operate to stay within the law while still maintaining profitability.

The State will attempt to stabilize business behavior around the Three Pillars through a combination of education, outreach, and law enforcement.

5.5. Implications for Foreign Policy

In the State system, the military leadership both informs and supports civilian decision-making at the National Council level. The foreign policy approach is to cultivate long-term relationships with whomever holds power in each foreign country. The strategy includes using our economic power to provide generous assistance, in part for humanitarian purposes, but also to strengthen ties with other governments and to foster international stability and prosperity, which are the foundation of lasting and meaningful peace - a precondition for our own sustainable independence and freedom.

We group foreign countries according to a few simple criteria, such as whether they are geographical neighbors, whether they are stable nation-states, and whether they hold positive or negative views about America. We then take a specific approach to relations with countries belonging to each category. This is elaborated in the Backgrounder on Internatonal Relations. Of course, each country is different, and some are different enough to require a customized approach to foreign relations.
