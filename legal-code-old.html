Legal Code

1. General Provisions

1.1. Structure of Statutes
This section concerns the structure of the code and identification of statutes.

1.1.1. Numbering
Entries in the legal code will be numbered using Arabic numerals in a way that represents a hierarchy of entries, with numerals separated by periods.

1.1.2. Transitional Period
Statutes pertaining to the transitional period when the United States is being shut down and the State is being established will contain the phrase, "Transfer Rule." Such statutes define exceptions or adjustments to standard law that will be in effect during the transitional period. After the transitional period, these statutes will no longer be relevant.

1.2. Use of Language
This section concerns the use of language in the statutes.

1.2.1. Language
Statutes will be written in American English as it is commonly used in and around the year 2022 CE.

1.2.2. Common-Sense Understanding
Statutes are to be interpreted according to their intended meaning to achieve justice, and not interpreted in a stilted or tricky way to win arguments on technicalities. In general, the spirit of the law takes precedence over the letter of the law.

1.2.3. Will and Shall
As the words will and shall have equivalent meaning, the word will is to be used. The word shall is considered obsolete.

1.2.4. Technical Terms
Technical words and phrases and those that have acquired a specific meaning in law will be construed according to such meaning.

1.2.5. Ambiguity Due to Tense and Gender
Present and future tenses are deemed equivalent; singular, plural, and categorical forms are deemed equivalent; and all grammatical genders are deemed equivalent for purposes of interpreting statutes.

1.2.6. Ambiguity Due to Conditional Language
The words could, should, and might are ambiguous and must not be used in statutes.

1.2.7. Intentionally-General Language
Words and phrases such as may, would, a normal person, reasonable, appropriate, and necessary are used in situations that call for judgment, and a static definition cannot apply to every circumstance.

1.2.8. Can, May, and Must
The word can implies ability rather than permission, so it will not be used in legal statutes.

The word may means "has the option to".
Example: "If the court rules in favor of plaintiff, plaintiff may seek financial compensation for damages." Plaintiff has the option to seek financial compensation.

The word must means "is required to".
Example: "When either counsel requests information from opposing counsel, opposing counsel must respond in writing within 15 calendar days of the request." Opposing counsel has no option to delay responding beyond 15 days.

1.2.9. Ambiguity Due to Punctuation
In a list of items, the Oxford comma will be used to avoid the possibility of interpreting the last two items in the list as being a pair or group that is considered as a single item.
Example: "Apples, oranges, pears, and lemons are fruits" is a list of four items; "Apples, oranges, pears and lemons" may be interpreted as a list of three items, in which "pears and lemons" together are treated as a single item.

1.3. Changes to the Legal Code
This section concerns the procedures to change the legal code.

1.3.1. Proposing a Change
A change to a legal statute can be proposed in any of the following ways:

  • Any member of the Decision Making Council can propose a change to the legal code.
  • The National Advisory Board, acting as a body, can propose a change to the legal code. The Board determines its own internal procedures for agreeing on a proposed change.
  • A proposed change can be submitted to the Decision Making Council through a citizen petition that garners at least 200,000 signature points.

1.3.2. Considering a Change
The Decision Making Council is required to consider and vote on any proposed change to the legal code that was presented through a method listed in section 1.3.1 within two (2) months of receipt of the proposal.

1.3.3. Passing a Change
A proposal that receives a minimum of twelve (12) votes in favor by the Decision Making Council is considered passed. The change does not have the force of law until it has been confirmed.

1.3.4. Confirming a Change
The National Advisory Board and the general public have six (6) months to review a change to the legal code that has been passed by the Decision Making Council. If anyone disagrees with the change, they must submit their argument against it within that time through one of the following methods:

  • Written notification to the Decision Making Council of a 75% majority vote against the change by the National Advisory Board.
  • Submission to the Decision Making Council of a citizen petition that garners at least 200,000 signature points.

1.3.5. Reconfirming a Change
A passed change that was not confirmed must be considered by the Decision Making Council within two (2) months of receipt of written notification of the agreement per section 1.3.4. After reconsidering the change, if the Council makes any adjustments to the proposed change, a new vote must be taken and the same procedures followed as for the first pass through the process. If the Council does not agree on any adjustments, the change is rejected and does not become law.

1.3.6. Discarding a Change
A change that was rejected by the people or the National Advisory Council, and that was not re-passed with adjustments by the Decision Making Council, is discarded and does not become law.

1.3.7. Limit on Re-Voting
A change that goes through the process twice without being confirmed or reconfirmed is discarded and does not become law.

1.3.8. Finalizing a Change
A change that is passed by the Decision Making Council and is not challenged within the period set forth in section 1.3.4 is considered finalized and becomes law as of the expiration date of the challenge period.

2. Humanity and the Law
The Constitution is largely based on human rights and the State's obligations to the people in that regard. This section of the legal code deals with definitions related to that subject.

2.1. Time Span For Human Rights
A person is entitled to human rights from the moment of conception until their affairs have been settled following their death.

Backgrounder: What Is Human?

2.1.2. Clarification: Abortion
This section does not create a cause of action for a woman who elects to abort a pregnancy with guidance from her doctor under conditions a normal person would consider reasonable or unavoidable. The human rights of an unborn child are equal to, but not greater than, the human rights of its mother or any other person. When unfortunate decisions must be made, it is possible no option will be available that fully supports everyone's rights.

2.1.3. Clarification: Murder of a Pregnant Person
A person who murders a pregnant woman may be charged with one additional count of murder for each unborn child the victim was carrying at the time of the murder.

2.1.4. Clarification: Unintended Most-Mortem Damage
This section does not create a cause of action for unintended or inadvertent slights to the reputation or good name of a deceased person during the time their final affairs are being put in order.

2.2. Human Rights as a Core Value
In case of conflict or apparent conflict between a statute and a person's human rights, the person's human rights will not be subverted.

3. State Services in Support of Human Needs
This section concerns obligations of the State toward the people with respect to human needs. The State may be required to provide for a need directly, or may be directed to establish the conditions to enable the people to provide for the need, or a combination.

Backgrounder: What Are Human Needs?

3.1. Physiological Needs
This section concerns the State's responsibilities to the people with respect to their physiological needs.

3.1.1. Universal Health Care
A single-payer health care system will be established, with the State acting in the roles of (a) standards-setter, (b) compliance monitor, and (c) single payer. Costs will be covered in part by an optional tax and in part by general income tax revenues.

3.1.2. Guaranteed Minimum Income
The State will establish a guaranteed minimum income benefit to be available to all citizens. Costs will be covered in part by an optional tax and in part by general income tax revenues.

3.1.3. General Retirement Program
The State will establish a retirement program to be available to all citizens. Costs will be covered in part by an optional tax and in part by general income tax revenues.

3.1.4. Incentives for Charitable Organizations
Organizations whose primary purpose is to provide services to people in need will be eligible for financial incentives. Both for-profit and non-profit organizations will be eligible for such incentives.

3.1.5. State Responsibility for Food Infrastructure
It is a responsibility of the State to maintain food production and distribution infrastructure adequately to ensure all people have access to sufficient basic foodstuffs for survival.

3.1.6. State Responsibility for Energy Infrastructure
It is a responsibility of the State to maintain energy production and distribution infrastructure adequately to ensure all people have adequate heating in winter.

3.2. Safety Needs
This section concerns the State's responsibilities to the people with respect to their safety needs.

3.2.1. National Defense
This section concerns the national defense aspect of support for safety needs.

3.2.1.1. State Responsibility for National Defense
It is a responsibility of the State to organize, manage, and fund national defense capabilities adequate to meet the challenges and threats of the time.

3.2.1.2. Citizen Responsibility for National Defense
It is a responsibility of each Citizen to serve (a) in the military or (b) in a non-violent community service capacity for three (3) years at some point between graduation from basic education and reaching the age of 45 years.

3.2.1.3. Citizen Qualification for Commissioned Officer Rank
Only Full Citizens are eligible to serve at a commissioned officer rank.

3.2.2. Law Enforcement
This section concerns the law enforcement aspect of support for safety needs.

3.2.2.1. Crime Investigation
Crime investigation is the primary function of law enforcement.

3.2.2.1.1. State Crime Investigation
It is a responsibility of the State to organize, fund, and operate investigative agencies as needed to deal with the needs of the people.

3.2.2.1.2. Local Crime Investigation
The State will offer organizational, operational, logistical, training, and supplemental funding support to local and regional law enforcement agencies to ensure they are able to serve the people adequately.

3.2.2.2. Crime Response
Crime response is the law enforcement function pertaining to real-time intervention in crimes in progress.

3.2.2.2.1. State Crime Response
The State will be prepared to deal with emergent situations that affect national security or the Three Pillars of Independence, either through dedicated specialized law enforcement agencies or by the use of military forces, or a combination.

3.2.2.2.2. Local Crime Response
The State will offer organizational, operational, logistical, training, and supplemental funding support to local and regional law enforcement agencies to ensure they are able to respond to emerging threat situations effectively. These are situations such as terrorist operations, hostage situations, and activities of similar scope.

3.2.2.3. Full Citizenship for Law Enforcement
Only Full Citizens can serve in a law enforcement occupation.

3.2.2.4. Policing
This subsection clarifies that policing and law enforcement are not the same. Policing is a method for authorities to monitor and control a population. It usually consists of officers wandering around and looking for trouble. The origins of policing in the US are connected with hunting down runaway slaves in the south and keeping immigrant populations out of white neighborhoods and suppressing organized labor in the cities of the north. In a civilized society, the people do not need to be "policed." We will assume our society is civilized unless and until the people demonstrate a need for stronger controls to ensure public safety.

3.2.2.5. Law Enforcement Interaction with Civilians
Law enforcement personnel are considered to have equal rights with all other Full Citizens. They are not afforded special rights to enable them to violate the law or subvert the human rights or legal rights of others, including suspects.

Clarifying note: The practice of "civil forfeiture" will be treated as criminal theft.

Clarifying note: The use of excessive violence, including killing unarmed suspects or suspects who have been brought under control, will be treated the same as if a civilian had exercised the violence.

Clarifying note: Law enforcement personnel cannot assert the right to lie to suspects while simultaneously requiring suspects to tell the truth.

Clarifying note: The fact an officer "followed procedure" when killing or harming a suspect or bystanders will not excuse the behavior, if a court determines the procedure itself violates human rights or legal rights. An officer's responsibility to uphold human rights and legal rights takes precedence over their obligation to follow prescribed procedures.

3.2.2.6. Civilian Interaction with Law Enforcement
Civilians are expected to cooperate with law enforcement personnel who are in the process of carrying out their duties, to the extent a normal person would consider reasonable and appropriate under the circumstances.

3.2.2.7. Disciplinary Action
This subsection concerns internal disciplinary actions within a law enforcement organization and the civilian oversight of such organizations.

3.2.2.7.1. Internal Disciplinary Action
Law enforcement organizations may create internal offices for the purpose of monitoring officer compliance with procedures and ethical guidelines and carrying out internal investigations of civilian complaints, internal reports of offenses, or their own observations of offenses.

3.2.2.7.2. LEOBR
The Law Enforcement Officers' Bill of Rights (LEOBR) of 1974 is adopted into this Legal Code.

Clarifying note: The combined effects of subsections 3.2.2.5 and 3.2.2.7.2 affect the ways in which suspects may be interrogated.

3.2.2.8. Public Oversight
A public review board may be convened to review decisions made by internal disciplinary organs of law enforcement organizations. The procedural details will be determined by the Decision Making Council.

3.2.3. Social Services
This section concerns the social services aspect of support for safety needs.

3.2.3.1. Social Services
The State will establish and operate organized services to assist people with issues such as domestic abuse, homelessness, and similar matters.

3.2.3.2. Tax Incentives
The State will offer tax incentives to encourage private entities to provide social services.

3.2.4. Emergency Response
This section concerns rapid response capability for emergencies such as fire, flood, volcanic eruption, and events of similar scope.

3.2.4.1. National Emergency Stockpiles
It is a responsibility of the State to identify needs and to organize, fund, monitor, and manage stockpiles of equipment and supplies that may be needed by emergency responders for events of large scale, such as hurricanes, floods, earthquakes, and so forth.

3.2.4.2. Support for Local Responders
It is a responsibility of the State to provide organizational, logistical, and training support and supplemental funding to support local and regional emergency response organizations.

3.2.5. Self Defense
This section concerns provisions for citizens to handle personal threat situations when conditions do not allow for calling the authorities.

3.2.5.1. Personal Weapons
Citizens are permitted to own and carry such weapons as a normal person would consider appropriate for the purpose of personal self-defense in common threat situations such as mugging, carjacking, home invasion, armed robbery, or acts of similar scope.

3.2.5.2. Use of Force
Citizens are authorized to use such force as a normal person would consider necessary to forestall an act of violence against themselves or others.

Clarifying note: This pertains to an immediate physical threat on a personal level, not to an abstract or convoluted rationalization to murder someone for political reasons to "prevent" some possible future act with which the killer disagrees.

3.2.5.3. Defense of Property
Unless an intruder uses or threatens violence as described in section 3.2.5.2, deadly force cannot be used to defend property or to deter or remove trespassers. The value of property does not rise to the level of the value of human life.

3.2.5.4. Abuse of Self-Defense Rights Is a Crime
An individual may be charged with a crime if they appear to have contrived an artificial threat situation as a way to commit murder under cover of self-defense.

3.2.5.5. Law Enforcement Exception
An individual is not authorized to exercise their self-defense rights against law enforcement officers who are carrying out their duty to question, detain, or arrest that individual or another individual.

3.2.5.6. Military Exception
An individual is not authorized to exercise their self-defense rights against military personnel carrying out their duties pursuant to the State's responsibilities to the nation. Under authorized and properly-announced conditions, people must follow the orders of military personnel. Under normal conditions, civilians are not under the command of military personnel.

3.2.5.7. Non-Citizens
Non-citizens are not authorized to carry weapons. Violators may be expelled from the country. There are no exceptions for diplomatic personnel. Visiting foreign military personnel may carry and use weapons only during training exercises.

3.2.6. State Regulatory Functions Pertaining to Safety
It is a responsibility of the State to define, monitor, and enforce standards pertaining to air quality, food safety, water quality, medicine quality and safety, workplace safety, and product safety.

3.3. The Need to Belong
This section concerns the State's responsibilities to the people with respect to their basic need to belong.

3.3.1. Socialization
It is a responsibility of the State to include positive socialization content or guidelines for content for

  • the standard public school curriculum;
  • core curriculum requirements for private schools; and
  • integration assistance for adult immigrants.

3.3.2. Incentives and Rewards for Service
It is a responsibility of the State to provide incentives and rewards to encourage Citizens to participate in

  • community service;
  • military service; and
  • law enforcement.

3.3.3. Bread and Circuses
It is a responsibility of the State to provide support and encouragement for circuses in the sense of "bread and circuses," to encourage a sense of belonging and to provide general contentment. This may include general entertainment, sporting events, festivals and celebrations, commemorations, and similar activities.

3.4. Growth Needs
This section concerns the State's responsibilities to the people with respect to growth needs.

3.4.1. Definitions
This subsection defines terms relevant to the statutes herein.

3.4.1.1. Definition
"Growth needs" are things people require in order to pursue their goals in life, above and beyond deficit needs.

3.4.1.2. Individuals and Corporations
Corporations are not considered "persons" under the law. Different rules may pertain to corporations than to individuals.

3.4.2. Individual Liberty
To pursue their goals in life, people need the individual liberty to live as they choose to live.

3.4.2.1. Spirit of the Law
The spirit of the law with respect to individual liberty is "everything is permitted, except what is specifically prohibited."

3.4.2.2. Limitations on Individual Liberty
It is a crime to subvert the individual liberty of another individual in pursuit of one's own individual liberty.

3.4.2.3. Three Pillars
It is a crime to use the concept of individual liberty as a cover to subvert the Three Pillars of Independence.

3.4.3. Private Property
To pursue their goals in life, people need the ability to own property.

3.4.3.1. Real Property Ownership
Citizens have the right to own real property anywhere within the State's jurisdiction. Non-citizens and foreigners are not permitted to own real property within the State's jurisdiction.

3.4.3.2. Movable Property Ownership
Everyone has the right to own movable property within the State's jurisdiction.

3.4.3.3. Intellectual Property Ownership
The creator of an original work or a work that represents a substantial innovation on existing work automatically owns the copyright to that work without the requirement to take any special action to register the copyright.

3.4.3.4. Patents
The creator of an original work or a work that represents a substantial innovation on existing work may apply for a patent for such work.

3.4.3.4.1. Patent Rights
A patent grants the owner the right to charge a fee for the use of the invention. Market forces will determine the amount of the fee except in the following cases:

  • the patent affects the cost of medicine, medical devices, or healthcare services that are covered as "basic care" under the national healthcare system; or
  • the patent affects the cost of infrastructure, resources, or procedures connected with the Three Pillars of Independence

In the cases listed above, the Decision Making Council may set maximum limits (caps) on patent fees.

3.4.3.4.2. Individual or Group Patent Owner
An individual inventor or group of inventors may apply for a patent.

3.4.3.4.3. Corporate Patent Owner
A company that has a pre-existing, written agreement with an individual inventor or group of inventors that cedes patent rights to the company may apply for a patent.

3.4.3.4.4. Patent Duration
A patent expires twenty (20) years after its approval date.

3.4.3.4.5. Patent Renewal
A patent cannot be renewed.

3.4.3.4.6. Patent Disputes
A patent owner may file legal action against an individual, group, or corporation they believe has profited from the use of their invention without paying the required fee or obtaining a written waiver of the fee from the patent owner. Courts will determine the validity of the claim.

3.4.3.4.7. International Patents
Patents registered in other countries will be honored within the jurisdiction of the State provided domestic patents are honored in those countries. Fees for the use of patented inventions are subject to the exceptions defined in section 3.4.3.4.1.

3.4.3.4.8. Patent Transfer Rule
A patent that was in effect in the United States prior to the establishment of the State is considered to remain in effect for twenty (20) years following the date it was originally approved.

3.4.4. Private Businesses
To pursue their goals in life, people need the ability to create and operate business entities.

3.4.4.1. Forming a Business
The intent of the law is that it be easy to form or dissolve a business entity. Forms and procedures for creating a business entity as defined by the Decision Making Council must consider simplicity as a basic requirement.

3.4.4.2. Corporate Income Tax Liability
Every business entity is subject to corporate income tax as defined in other sections of this code.

3.4.4.3. Registration Fee
Every business entity must pay an annual registration fee to be recognized as a legal business entity. The amount or schedule of amounts is set by the Decision Making Council. It is the intent of the law that the amount be proportional to the size of the business.

3.4.4.4. Three Pillars of Independence
Any business entity that intentionally or knowingly subverts the Three Pillars of Independence may forfeit its right to exist and be terminated by the State. In addition, its leaders may be prosecuted. This applies to (a) domestic businesses whose operating philosophy is "company over country," and (b) foreign-owned businesses that are used as an instrument of espionage or influence over the State or its people. In the event a foreign-owned business is convicted of violating this statute, all its physical and intellectual property assets located in or developed within the jurisdiction of the State are subject to confiscation, and its employees who are not citizens of the State may be expelled from the country.

3.4.4.5. Price Controls
History has demonstrated that a national economy cannot be managed centrally. Therefore, the State will generally allow the natural operation of supply and demand to determine the price of goods and the cost of labor. However, the State reserves the right to step in should prices or wages be set by a private entity in a way that subverts the human needs, human rights, or legal rights of individuals.

3.4.4.6. Proportion of Citizen Employees
Seventy (70) percent or more of the total number of workers in any corporation operating within the State's jurisdiction must be citizens of the State. "Workers" includes every person engaged in performing work on behalf of the business - full time employees, contract workers, both hourly and exempt employees, and executive management. "Work on behalf of the business" includes work performed in person at a physical location within the State's jurisdiction or elsewhere, and work performed remotely by people located anywhere in the universe.

3.4.4.7. Conditions for Visitors to Businesses
Owners or the designated operators of private businesses may set conditions for people to visit the premises to conduct business. Private businesses are not public spaces. Everyone does not automatically have blanket permission to enter private businesses and do as they please while there.

Clarifying note: Such conditions may include anything a normal person would consider reasonable in context. Examples include but are not limited to: (a) safety gear - hard hats, radiation badges, medical face masks, clean suits; (b) footwear - boots or sturdy shoes vs. sandals; (c) clothing - "no shoes, no shirt, no service" (d) limitations on the number of people who can occupy a given space, (e) limitations on the length of time visitors may remain on the premises, (f) limitations on items visitors are permitted to carry on the premises - flammable compounds, weapons, communication devices; (g) limitations on the presence of animals on the premises - "service animals only"; (h) limitations on noise - be quiet in a library or in a hospital zone; (i) limitations on making recordings - no photos, video, or audio recording; (j) limitations on touching items on the premises - do not touch the art works or historical artifacts in the museum, or the equipment in the lab; (k) personal cleanliness; (l) obvious health issues that may constitute a risk to others; (m) courteous behavior; and other considerations of similar scope.

Limitations: Such conditions must not violate human rights or legal rights.

3.4.4.8. Regulatory Compliance
Businesses that operate in economic sectors affecting the Three Pillars of Independence, or whose operations may potentially damage the natural environment or create risk to workers and visitors, or whose operations may potentially defraud customers or partners, or whose operations may create risk to the general public, must comply with regulations established by the relevant State agencies.

3.4.4.9. Accessibility
Businesses that serve the public must ensure all members of the public are physically able to conduct business safely and with a level of convenience a normal person would consider appropriate in context.

3.4.4.10. Accountability
The individuals who form and operate a business are responsible for any criminal charges or civil litigation against that business. If the business is dissolved after charges or suits are filed against it, the individuals who were responsible for the business remain accountable.

Clarifying note: The spirit of the law is that people cannot use business entities as a cover for their own illicit activities, or to shift legal or financial responsibility for harm away from themselves. This statute will be so interpreted in court.

3.4.4.11. Business Transfer Rule
A business established in the United States prior to the establishment of the State is considered to continue in existence. There are no formal procedures to continue the operation of the business. Such businesses will register with the State no later than the first day of the tax year following the establishment of the State. Failure to register will incur financial penalties, but no criminal prosecution.

3.4.5. Education
To pursue their goals in life, people need a basic level of knowledge and skills. For some occupations, additional training or education is needed on top of basic knowledge.

Backgrounder: Education

3.4.5.1. State's Role
The State has the obligation to establish and maintain the conditions that enable people to obtain the basic knowledge and skills necessary for them to pursue their goals in life.

3.4.5.2. Public Schools
The State may establish and operate schools, supported by tax revenues, to provide people with basic education.

3.4.5.3. Private and Home Schools
The State may define a core curriculum to be used in private schools and home schools. Private and home schools are free to add information to the curriculum, but not to remove any content from the core curriculum.

Clarifying note: The spirit of the law is that the core curriculum represent a minimum set of knowledge and skills necessary for a person to function as an adult member of society, including basic reading, writing, and math skills and the State's responsibilities defined under section 3.3.1 of this code. It is not intended to be an all-encompassing, comprehensive curriculum. The expectation is that educational institutions and home schoolers will supplement the core curriculum with enriched content.

3.4.5.4. Public Trade Schools
The State may establish and operate trade schools to prepare citizens for work in the trades, following their basic education.

3.4.5.5. Private Trade Schools
Private business entities may establish and operate trade schools.

3.4.5.6. Public Universities
The State may establish and operate universities to prepare citizens for work in the professions, following their basic education.

3.4.5.7. Private Universities
Private business entities may establish and operate universities and specialized institutions of higher education. The State does not set standards or monitor performance of such institutions. It is expected that the market will judge the value of the education provided.

3.4.5.8. Educational Institutions and Rights
All educational institutions, public and private, will respect the human rights and legal rights of the people. Anyone academically qualified may attend any learning institution. Violators will be subject to corrective action and oversight for a defined period of time. Repeat violators may be subject to being shut down.

3.4.5.9. Educational Institutions and the Three Pillars
No educational institution, public or private, will promote the subversion of the Three Pillars of Independence. Offending institutions will be shut down and their leaders prosecuted.

3.4.5.10. Details Out of Scope for Statutory Law
The details of basic school duration, schedules, curriculum content, and other operational matters are out of scope for this code, and will be set by the Decision Making Council.

Clarifying note: These are considered to be operational details that must be easier to change than statutory law.

4. Taxes and Services
This section defines the various taxes and the services they support.

4.1. Income Tax
This subsection concerns income taxes.

4.1.1. Income Tax Rates
Income tax rates are out of scope for statutory law and will be set by the State as regulations.

4.1.2. Income Tax Rate Changes
Income tax rates may be changed by the Decision Making Council. The public must be given advance notice of at least eighteen (18) months for any change to income tax rates. Changes in rates must go into effect on the first day of a tax year.

4.1.3. Income Tax Rate Transfer Rule
During the transitional period when the State is established to replace the United States of America, the income tax rate will be zero until the Decision Making Council sets the initial income tax rates for the new government. It is the intent of this statute that the transitional period be no longer than one year. The State will take no action at any lateer time to attempt to collect income taxes for the transitional period.

4.2. Individual Income Taxes and Services
This subsection concerns individual income taxes.

4.2.1. Basic Income Tax
This subsection concerns the basic income tax.

4.2.1.1. Purpose
The purpose of the basic income tax is to provide general revenue for State operation.

4.2.1.2. Mandatory
The basic income tax is mandatory.

4.2.2. Healthcare Tax
This subsection concerns the healthcare tax.

4.2.2.1. Purpose
The purpose of the healthcare tax is to provide revenue to support the single payer healthcare system. Revenues are put into the general fund, not a dedicated fund.

4.2.2.2. Optional
The healthcare tax is optional. Citizens may opt in or out of the healthcare system on an annual basis at the time they file their income tax returns.

4.2.2.3. Eligibility for Services
Citizens who opt into the healthcare system will be eligible for healthcare services during years when they opt in. In addition, citizens younger than the age of majority, and citizens who have officially retired are eligible for healthcare services.

4.2.2.4. Healthcare Services Transfer Rule
During the transitional period all residents within the State's jurisdiction will be eligible for basic healthcare services. The intent of this statute is to avoid causing health issues for anyone while the new system is being established.

4.2.3. Guaranteed Minimum Income Tax
This subsection concerns the guaranteed minimum income tax.

4.2.3.1. Purpose
The purpose of the guaranteed minimum income tax is to provide revenue to support the guaranteed minimum income service. Revenues are put into the general fund, not a dedicated fund.

4.2.3.2. Optional
The guaranteed minimum income tax is optional. Citizens may opt in or out of the guaranteed minimum income benefit on an annual basis at the time they file their income tax returns.

4.2.3.3. Eligibility for Services
Citizens who opt into the guaranteed minimum income tax will be eligible for guaranteed minimum income payments during the years they opt in.

4.2.3.4. Guaranteed Minimum Income Transfer Rule
During the transitional period no guaranteed minimum income will be paid.

4.2.4. Retirement Tax
This subsection concerns the retirement tax.

4.2.4.1. Purpose
The purpose of the retirement tax is to provide revenue to support the retirement service. Revenues are put into the general fund, not a dedicated fund.

4.2.4.2. Optional
The retirement tax is optional. Citizens may opt in or out of the retirement system on an annual basis at the time they file their income tax returns.

4.2.4.3. Eligibility for Services
Citizens who opt into the retirement tax will be eligible for retirement benefit payments at a level corresponding to the number of years they opted into the retirement tax.

4.2.4.4. Minimum Retirement Age
Citizens may file for the retirement benefit in the calendar year in which they turn 55 years of age, or any year thereafter.

4.2.4.5. Retirement Benefit Transfer Rule
This subsection concerns the transition of United States Social Security benefits and contributions to the State system.

4.2.4.5.1. Social Security Recipients
Citizens who are collecting United States Social Security benefits at the time of the transition will continue to receive the same amount throughout the transition and after establishment of the State for the remainder of their lives.

4.2.4.5.2. Social Security Contributors
Each year a citizen paid into the Social Security system will count as a year of eligibility for the State retirement benefit.

4.2.4.5.3. Private Pension Funds
The transition to the State system will have no effect on private pension/retirement funds.

4.3. Corporate Income Taxes and Services
This subsection concerns corporate income taxes.

4.3.1. Purpose
The purpose of the corporate income tax is to provide revenue to support the Three Pillars of Independence and the State's obligations to the people.

4.3.2. Mandatory
The corporate income tax is mandatory.

4.3.3. Scope
The corporate income tax applies to operations within the State's jurisdiction by entities formed within the State or elsewhere. When an entity has operations both within the State's jurisdiction and elsewhere, only those operations within the State's jurisdiction are in scope for the corporate income tax.

4.3.4. Tax Offset for Public Services
Entities that perform public services such as sponsoring temporary housing for the homeless, soup kitchens for the hungry, or other public services of similar scope and purpose, will have their corporate income tax liability offset by the amount they spend in providing public services.

Clarifying note: There is no specific tax exemption for religious orders, as there must be strict separation of religion and government. However, if a religious order provides public services, it can enjoy the same tax offset as any other corporate entity.

4.4. Value Add Tax
This subsection concerns the value add tax on transactions.

4.4.1. Purpose
Revenues collected through the value add tax (VAT) go into the general operating fund of the State.

4.4.2. Rate
The Decision Making Council will set one rate or separate rates for wholesale and retail transactions, at its discretion. The Council may, at its discration, define different rules for the sale of goods and the sale of services, or other categories of transactions.

4.4.3. Rate Changes
The value add tax rate(s) may be changed by the Decision Making Council. The public must be given a minimum of twelve (12) calendar months advance warning before a rate change goes into effect.

4.4.4. National Advisory Board Objection
The National Advisory Board may raise an objection to a change in the value add tax rate within two (2) weeks of the announcement of the change.

4.4.5. Public Objection
The general public may raise an objection to a change in the value add tax rate within six (6) months of the announcement of the change by submitting a petition with 200,000 or more signature points. If a member of the public wishes to circulate a petition, they must notify the Decision Making Council of their intent within two (2) weeks of the announcement of the change. If 200,000 signature points cannot be obtained within the six (6) month petition period, the objection is null and void. While a petition is in circulation, or until the expiration of the six (6) month period, the approval process for the rate change is suspended.

4.4.6. Single Objection
When an objection is registered, the Decision Making Council is required to debate the objection publicly and reach a decision within three (3) calendar months of receiving notice of the objection. One objection and subsequent debate is permitted. The twelve (12) month warning period begins after the objection has been heard and the decision is finalized.

4.4.7. Value-Add Tax Transfer Rule
The United States has no Value-Add tax, but there are state and local sales taxes. The existing state and local taxes will continue in effect during the transitional period, but the revenues will go into the State's general operating fund. Once the Decision Making Council has set rates for the Value-Add Tax, it will replace the previous sales taxes. It is the intent of this statute that the transition take no longer than one year.

Clarifying note: State and local taxing authorities will continue to use the bank accounts they currently use for managing revenues collected through sales taxes until the State has established operating accounts for this purpose. At that time, monies may be transferred to the new accounts. The intent is to avoid creating confusion, logistical problems, or funding problems during the transitional period.

4.5. Infrastructure Maintenance Fee
This subsection concerns the infrastructure maintenance fee.

4.5.1. Purpose
Revenues collected for infrastructure maintenance will be used to maintain general infrastructure necessary to support the Three Pillars of Independence.

4.5.2. Proportionality
The infrastructure maintenance fee is a use fee. Individuals and businesses that use infrastructure will be charged proportionally per their use.

4.5.3. Rates
The rate or rates for infrastructure maintenance fees will be set by the Decision Making Council.

4.5.4. Rate Changes
Infrastructure maintenance rates may be changed by the Decision Making Council. The public must be given a minimum of twelve (12) calendar months before a rate change goes into effect.

4.6. Three Pillars Sustainability Adjustment
This subsection concerns the Three Pillars sustainability adjustment.

4.6.1. Purpose
In the event the Decision Making Council determines the current fees or charges pertaining to public or private operations affecting the Three Pillars of Independence are leading to a degradation in the State's ability to sustain the Three Pillars, the Council may institute temporary upward or downward adjustments of specific fees or taxes for public services or businesses that affect the sustainability of the Three Pillars of Independence.

4.6.2. Changes in the Adjustment
The Three Pillars Sustainability adjustment may be changed by the Decision Making Council. The public must be given a minimum of twelve (12) calendar months before a change goes into effect, if the change results in an increase in fees. Changes that result in a decrease in fees may be put into effect immediately upon approval by the Decision Making Council, or following any public challenges to the change, whichever is later.

4.6.4. Automatic Cancellation
Absent specific action to change or extend the adjustment, the Three Pillars sustainability adjustment will automatically expire after three (3) calendar years of effect.

4.6.4. Extension of the Adjustment
When an adjustment is due to expire, the Decision Making Council must proactively solicit a public referendum at least six (6) months prior to the expiry date to obtain general public approval for the extension. In no case can an extension exceed twelve (12) months in duration.

4.6.5. Limit of Extensions
An adjustment can be extended once. If the Decision Making Council desires the adjustment to continue beyond that time, it must initiate the procedure to approve a new adjustment. The new adjustment is not considered as an extension to the expired one.

4.7. Property Tax
This section concerns taxes on real property owned.

4.7.1. Standard Real Property Tax
A rate to be determined by the Decision Making Council will be due and payable at the end of each tax year, based on the acreage of the property.

4.7.2. Three Pillars Adjustment for Real Property Tax
Property that is actively used for the purpose of supporting one or more of the Three Pillars of Independence will be taxed at a lower rate than the standard rate, to be determined by the Decision Making Council.

4.7.3. Property Tax Transfer Rule
The United States has no national property tax, but there are state and local property taxes. The existing state and local taxes will continue in effect during the transitional period, but the revenues will go into the State's general operating fund. Once the Decision Making Council has set rates for property taxes, these will replace the previous state and local property taxes. It is the intent of this statute that the transition take no longer than one year.

Clarifying note: State and local taxing authorities will continue to use the bank accounts they currently use for managing revenues collected through property taxes until the State has established operating accounts for this purpose. At that time, monies may be transferred to the new accounts. The intent is to avoid creating confusion, logistical problems, or funding problems during the transitional period.

4.8. Under- and Over-Payment of Taxes
When the amount of tax paid differs from the amount due, an adjustment will be made for the tax year following the year in which the error was detected. In the event this would create an undue financial burden on the taxpayer, a payment plan will be agreed. The State is not permitted to refuse a taxpayer's request to set up a payment plan.

Clarifying note: Refunds to taxpayers will not increase the recipients tax liability; they will not be counted as income.

4.9. Tax Fraud
Taxpayers who defraud the State may be enrolled in a payment plan to catch up with missed tax payments. In the event the effects of the fraud cause financial or other loss to people other than the taxpayer who commits the fraud, additional criminal penalties may apply depending on the judgment of the courts.

4.10. Tax Audits
This section concerns audits of tax payments.

4.10.1. State's Right to Audit
The State may carry out audits of taxpayers, both individuals and corporations, on a random basis to spot-check compliance. The Decision Making Council will establish rules and procedures for tax audits.

4.10.2. Audit Moratorium
For a period of five (5) years beginning with the start of the transition from the United States to the State, no individual or corporate tax returns will be subject to audit.

Clarifying note: It is expected that there will be confusion and mistakes during the transitional period. The cost of auditing and making corrections is likely to be greater than the value of correcting these mistakes. There will be many other structural and procedural changes to make during the transition that are of higher importance.

4.11. Tax Account Transfer Rules
Any tax payments made to the government of the United States or other domestic tax authorities prior to the establishment of the State will be applied toward individual and corporate tax liability to the State. Any monies held by a government agency in excess of an individual's or corporation's tax liability to the State will be refunded in full no later than the end of the first full tax year of State operation. Monies not in excess of an individual's or corporation's tax liability to the State will apply toward payment of such taxes, either in the first full tax year or in partial payments over a series of years, at the taxpayer's option.

5. Citizenship
This section defines citizenship and the legal basis for obtaining or changing citizenship status.

5.1. Levels of Citizenship
This subsection concerns the various level of citizenship.

5.1.1. Full Citizenship
This subsection defines the requirements and obligations of Full Citizenship.

5.1.1.2. Requirements
This subsection defines the requirements for Full Citizenship.

5.1.1.2.1. Full Investment in the System
To qualify for Full Citizenship, a citizen must be fully invested in the system as defined by their opting into all optional income taxes.

5.1.1.2.2. Living Within the Law
To qualify for Full Citizenship, a citizen must not be serving a sentence for a crime. After completing such a sentence, the citizen must live lawfully for one calendar year following the end of their sentence before obtaining or regaining Full Citizenship status for purposes of voting and participation in the government. They may obtain Full Citizenship status for purposes of tax-supported services immediately upon completion of their sentence.

5.1.1.3. Obligations and Privileges
This subsection specifies the obligations and privileges of Full Citizenship.

5.1.1.3.1. Participation in Voting
A Full Citizen who has reached the age of majority must cast a vote in all matters for which they are eligible to vote. Failure to cast a vote will result in reduction of voting strength and petition signature power to one (1) point for a period of twelve (12) calendar months.

5.1.1.3.2. Voting Strength
Each vote by a Full Citizen of the professional class has a force of three (3) points. Each signature by a Full Citizen of the professional class on a petition to the State has a force of three (3) points.

Each vote by a Full Citizen of the vocational class has a force of two (2) points. Each signature by a Full Citizen of the vocational class on a petition to the State has a force of two (2) points.

5.1.1.3.3. State Service
A Full Citizen of the professional class may serve on the National Advisory Board and the Decision Making Council. In the event a member of the National Advisory Board or the Decision Making Council loses Full Citizenship status, their position in the State body is immediately terminated. After recovering Full Citizenship, they may run for membership in the National Advisory Board again, subject to any other rules or regulations of that body.

5.1.1.3.4. Military Rank
A Full Citizen may serve as a commissioned officer in the military. There is no class requirement. In the event a serving officer loses Full Citizenship, their commission is immediately terminated. After recovering Full Citizenship, they may follow standard procedures for promotion or appointment to a commissioned rank.

5.1.1.3.5. Law Enforcement Service
A Full Citizen may work in the law enforcement field. There is no class requirement. In the event a law enforcement officer loses Full Citizenship, they are immediately ineligible for field work and for any job that requires carrying a weapon, until they have recovered Full Citizenship. There will be no reduction in rank or pay.

5.1.1.3.6. Court Judge
A Full Citizen of the professional class may work as a court judge. In the event a serving judge loses Full Citizenship, they must cease working as a judge until they have recovered Full Citizenship status.

5.1.1.3.7. Attorney
A Full Citizen of the professional class may work as an attorney, subject to other rules established by the legal profession. In the event an attorney loses Full Citizenship, they must cease working as an attorney until they have recovered Full Citizenship status.

5.1.2. Basic Citizenship
This subsection defines the requirements and obligations of Basic Citizenship.

5.1.2.2. Definition
Out of respect for different philosophical views regarding the proper role of government, everyone is free to opt out of selected parts of the system, including (a) healthcare services, (b) guaranteed minimum income payments, and (c) retirement program. As this choice reduces the individual's investment in the system, the law provides that their voice in government is proportionally reduced, compared with that of a Full Citizen. As the Basic Citizen is not fully invested in the system, they are restricted from working in certain occupations or serving in certain State roles, as they may not have the same dedication to the country as a Full Citizen.

5.1.2.3. Loss of Full Citizenship Status
This subsection defines how a Full Citizen may become a Basic Citizen, and how Full Citizenship can be restored.

5.1.2.3.1. Criminal Conviction
A Full Citizen who is convicted of a crime is reduced to Basic Citizenship status for the duration of their sentence. After completing their sentence, the individual must live for one calendar year without any new criminal convictions. At that time, they are eligible for Full Citizenship status provided they follow the usual rules for such status. They may opt into the optional tax-supported services immediately on completing their sentence, but their voting power will not be restored until they have completed one year living within the law.

5.1.2.3.2. Opting Out of the System
A Full Citizen who opts out of one or more optional income tax categories is reduced to Basic Citizenship status unless and until they choose to opt back into all tax categories in a future tax year. Citizens may opt in or out of these tax categories at the beginning of each tax year, but not in the middle of a tax year. There is no limitation on the number of times an individual may opt in or out of any or all optional income tax categories.

5.1.2.4. Inviolability
No one's basic citizenship can be taken from them.

5.1.2.4.1. Criminal Conviction
The State cannot remove anyone's citizenship entirely, including in case of criminal conviction, even for the crimes of sedition or treason.

5.1.2.4.2. Revocation of Citizenship
This subsection pertains to the voluntary revocation of State citizenship and the rules for reinstatement of citizenship.

5.1.2.4.2.1. Voluntary Revocation of Citizenship
A citizen residing abroad may revoke their State citizenship. This may be required by their country of residence, or the individual may consider it advantageous. While their citizenship is revoked, the individual will have no tax liability to the State, will receive no State benefits, and will have no right to vote or sign citizen petitions.

Clarifying note: This applies to the retirement benefit. Retirement payments will cease when a citizen revokes their State citizenship. Those wishing to retire abroad should consider this before choosing the locale for their retirement, or before deciding to revoke for some other reason.

5.1.2.4.2.2. Reinstatement of Revoked Citizenship
The State will reinstate citizenship upon request. There are no additional requirements to regain revoked citizenship. No processing fee will be charged for this transaction.

Clarifying note: Revocation of citizenship is for outward appearances, to satisfy a foreign law while living abroad, or as an expression of dissent. As far as the State is concerned, "once a citizen, always a citizen."

5.1.2.5. Privileges
This subsection defines the privileges of Basic Citizenship.

5.1.2.5.1. Participation in Voting
A Basic Citizen who has reached the age of majority may cast a vote in all matters for which they are eligible to vote.

5.1.2.5.2. Voting Strength
Each vote by a Basic Citizen of any class has a force of one (1) point. Each signature by a Basic Citizen on a petition to the State has a force of one (1) point.

5.1.2.5.3. State Service
A Basic Citizen is not eligible to serve on the National Advisory Board or the Decision Making Council.

5.1.2.5.4. Military Rank
A Basic Citizen may serve in the military at a noncommissioned rank.

5.1.2.5.5. Restricted Occupations
A Basic Citizen may not serve in law enforcement, as a court judge, or as an attorney. Exception: Law enforcement personnel who were Full Citizens when hired and later were changed to Basic Citizens (see 5.1.1.3.5).

5.1.2.6. Multiple Citizenship
An individual who holds citizenship concurrently in the State and in one or more foreign countries cannot become a Full Citizen of the State for purposes of voting and participation in government, as they have divided loyalty by definition. They are still eligible for tax-supported services if they opt in to the optional taxes for those services.

5.2. Obtaining Citizenship
This subsection concerns the ways a person can obtain citizenship.

5.2.1. Citizenship By Parentage
The natural child or grandchild of a citizen is considered to be a citizen by parentage.

5.2.2. Citizenship By Surrogate Parentage
A child born to a non-citizen surrogate mother on behalf of a citizen is considered to be a citizen by parentage, unless the surrogate mother refuses to release the child.

5.2.3. Citizenship By Adoption
The adopted child of a citizen is considered to be a citizen by parentage, provided adoption occurs before the age of majority.

5.2.4. Citizenship By Cloning
The cloned child of a citizen is considered to be a citizen by parentage.

5.2.5. Citizenship By Choice
A person who follows standard procedures to obtain legal residency status in the country and lives lawfully for the required time may apply for citizenship. If the application is accepted, the person is considered to be a citizen by choice.

5.2.6. Citizenship By Transfer
A person who was a citizen of the United States of America at the time the State was established is automatically considered a citizen of the State.

5.2.7. Same Rights and Obligations
All citizens have the same rights and obligations, regardless of how they obtained citizenship.

5.2.8. Default Status
Regardless of the method by which citizenship is obtained, every new citizen is considered a Full Citizen by default.

5.2.9. Changing Citizenship Status
Upon reaching the age of majority, at the time of filing annual income taxes, the individual may opt out of optional income taxes and the services they fund. The change goes into effect starting in the new tax year.

5.2.10. Multiple Citizenship
An individual may obtain State citizenship without renouncing prior citizenship in another country. In that case, the individual will always be a Basic Citizen and cannot become a Full Citizen (see section 5.1.2.5.6). If the individual opts into any or all optional income taxes, they are eligible for the associated State services.

6. Class
This subsection concerns the class structure of the State.

6.1. Professional Class
Members of the professional class have completed formal education at least through the Bachelor's Degree level. They have some privileges not extended to the vocational class, as defined in other statutes.

6.1.1. Domestic Higher Educational Credentials
University credentials at the Bachelor level obtained in an accredited educational institution in the State's jurisdiction are automatically recognized for the purpose of defining class. There are no limitations regarding major subject area and no minimum grade requirement.

6.1.2. Foreign Higher Educational Credentials
University credentials obtained in a foreign educational institution may be recognized by the State after an inquiry into the quality of the credentials.

6.1.3. Transferred Higher Educational Credentials
University credentials obtained in the United States prior to the establishment of the State will be recognized.

6.1.4. Special Dispensation
In some cases, a person may have a professional track record or may have demonstrated outstanding ability in the arts, but does not qualify for the professional class due to a technicality. In such cases, the individual may request the State to grant professional class status by special dispensation. The Decision Making Council will define the requirements for special dispensation.

6.2. Vocational Class
Everyone who has not completed formal education at least through the Bachelor's degree level, or demonstrated equivalent knowledge, is considered to belong to the vocational class.

6.2.1. Default Class
A person who has not demonstrated qualification for the professional class will be considered a member of the vocational class by default. Individuals whose foreign university credentials are under review is considered to belong to the vocational class while the review is underway.

6.2.2. Foreign Basic Educational Credentials
Basic school credentials obtained in a foreign educational institution may be recognized by the State. In case such credentials are not recognized, the individual may take adult education courses and/or pass one or more examinations to gain the credential.

6.3. Class Mobility
This subsection concerns changing from the vocational to the professional class.

6.3.1. From Vocational to Professional Class
A person who joined the vocational class after completing basic education, and who later decides to change careers, may change to the professional class upon completing the necessary educational requirements.

6.3.2. From Professional to Vocational Class
A person who joined the professional class and who later decides to change careers to work in the trades may change careers without forfeiting their professional class status.

6.3.3. Failed Professional
A person who joined the professional class and cannot perform adequately in a professional role, or who is convicted of an ethics violation, may be converted to the vocational class. The intent of the law is that the standard to prove incompetence is very high. This statute is not to be used to punish people for disagreement or other personal reasons. Abuses of this statute may be prosecuted.

7. Age of Majority
The age of majority is the age at which a person is treated as an adult for legal purposes.

7.1. Adult Age
A person is considered an adult for legal purposes at midnight, or 00:00 hours, Central Standard Time, on their 16th birthday.

Limitations: As a 16-year-old will not have completed the minimum educational requirements, it will not be possible for them to join the professional class or serve on the National Advisory Board until later in life. All other obligations and privileges of adulthood will apply.

Backgrounder: Age of Majority

8. Households
A household is a set of adults and their children who are organized as a family unit.

8.1. No Forced Separation
Children cannot be separated from their households arbitrarily or for political reasons under any circumstances. This applies to citizens, including those serving sentences for criminal confictions; non-citizens; refugees; asylum-seekers; and legal or illegal immigrants.

8.2. Abuse or Neglect of Children
The State may separate children from their parents only in cases of abuse or neglect, following due process of law.

8.3. Household Composition and Organization
The State does not impose rules on the composition or organization of a household.

8.4. Limitation on Household Size
To avoid unintended use of this law, the State imposes a maximum size of four (4) adults and twelve (12) children in any single household. Exceptions may be granted on a case-by-case basis.

8.5. Registration
To ensure household members are able to exercise their rights, a household must be registered with the State.

8.6. Unregistration
Households may be unregistered by agreement of its adult members.

8.7. Joining a Household
An adult individual may join a registered household.

8.7.1. Adult Joining a Household
An adult may join a registered household by following a simple administrative procedure, subject to the limitation defined in section 8.9.

8.7.2. Minor Joining a Household
A minor who is born into a household or formally adopted by a household becomes a member of that household.

8.7.3. Minor Changing Household
A minor cannot live separately from a household. When households break up and re-form with different members, minors must choose which household they wish to care for them.

8.8. Withdrawing from a Household
Individuals may withdraw from a household.

8.8.1. Adult Withdrawal from Household
An adult member of a household may withdraw from that household.

8.8.2. Minor Withdrawal from Household
A minor member of a household may petition the relevant State agency to review their case for withdrawal from their household.

8.9. Single Household Membership
No one may belong to more than one household at the same time.

8.10. Household Member Rights and Responsibilities
Household members have special rights and responsibilities with respect to

  • guardianship of minors;
  • hospital visitation;
  • living wills;
  • default inheritance rules; and
  • default powers of attorney during health crises.

8.11. Distinct from Religion
Households as recognized by the State are distinct from marriages or family units as recognized by religious orders.

Clarifying note: Religious marriages are not automatically recognized as households for legal purposes. To ensure the legal rights of their members, they must register as households. (See sections 8.5 and 8.7). Similarly, withdrawal from a household or unregistration of the household has no effect on the status of a marriage in the eyes of a religious tradition.

8.12. Fee Waiver
The intent of the law is that anyone is able to register with a household and to dissolve such a household easily. The first registration and first unregistration (if any) will be carried out with no fee. Subsequent registrations and unregistrations will incur a minimal processing fee, not to be set so high as to discourage people from using the process. Individuals who join a household or withdraw from a household will be charged a minimal processing fee.

8.13. Household Transfer Rule
United States law does not have the concept of a household. People who lived as families prior to the establishment of the State may continue to live together, but they must register as households to get the benefits of State law. The State does not interfere with any adult's choice to live with other people, but such an arrangement provides no special privileges unless it is registered as a household. Minors cannot live separately from a household (see section 8.7.3). Therefore, families that have one or more minor children must register as households within one year of the beginning of State operation.

9. Minors
This section concerns adults' responsibilities to uphold the rights of minors.

9.1. Definitions
Pursuant to section 2.1 of this code, individuals are entitled to human rights and legal rights from the moment of conception until their affairs are finalized following physical death. A "minor" is defined as anyone who has not reached the age of majority. The "pre-birth" stage of development is the interval between conception and birth. The "childhood" stage is the interval between birth and the age of majority.

9.2. Rights of Minors
As minors are not equipped to defend their own rights, all adults living in the jurisdiction of the State are responsible for upholding the human and legal rights of minors. This responsibility falls to all adults, whether citizens or not, whether residents or visitors, whether the adults are part of the same household as the children, and regardless of any special considerations such as diplomatic immunity.

9.3. Rights During the Pre-Birth Stage
The human and legal rights of minors in the pre-birth stage of development are equal to those of any other people. However, they cannot make decisions for themselves. The primary responsibility for decisions affecting the health and well-being of individuals in the pre-birth stage falls to the pregnant woman.

The State understands that under certain circumstances, people must make difficult choices, including (a) the decision to abort the pregnancy, or (b) the decision to carry the pregnancy despite health risks to the mother and child, possibly against the advice of doctors. The State will not interfere in such choices. When abortion is decided based on conditions a normal person would consider reasonable, this statute will not create a cause for action against the pregnant woman or anyone in her household or her medical team. Inadvertent termination of the pregnancy in the normal course of events, such as a miscarriage, will not be treated as a criminal act.

9.4. Rights During the Childhood Stage
Minors have the same human and legal rights as adults, but lack the capacity to defend their own rights.

9.4.1. Adult Responsibilities Toward Minors
Every adult has the obligation to assist and guide minors when they find a minor in a situation that calls for assistance or guidance. Failure to do so is a crime. When an adult intentionally causes harm to or manipulates a minor, the penalties will exceed those for similar crimes carried out against other adults. Failure to report offenses against minors is a crime.

9.4.2. Adult Offenses Against Minors
When an adult intentionally causes harm to or manipulates or takes advantage of a minor, the penalties will exceed those for similar crimes carried out against other adults. Failure to report offenses against minors is a crime.

9.4.3. Obligation to Report Offenses
Failure to report offenses against minors is a crime.

9.5. Offenses Committed By Minors
This subsection defines special considerations when an offender is a minor.

9.5.1. General Offenses
Offenses by minors that do not result in harm to others or significant financial losses will be treated as opportunities for correction and guidance rather than for punishment.

9.5.2. Heinous Offenses
Heinous offenses by minors, such as rape, torture, mutilation, dismemberment, brain damage, loss of physical function, murder, or crimes of similar scope, will be treated the same as if they had been committed by adults. There is no minimum age for offenders in this category.

9.5.3. Right to Recover Financial Losses
In the event an offense committed by a minor results in significant financial losses for the victim(s), the victim(s) or their survivors may seek restitution from the household(s) responsible for raising the perpetrators. If the perpetrators were in the care of the State at the time of the offense, then the State may be liable for restoring the victim(s).

9.6. Adoption
This subsection concerns the laws governing adoption of minors.

9.6.1. Background Checks and Probationary Period
All subsections under 9.6 are subject to the stipulations in this subsection.

9.6.1.1. Background Checks
All adults involved in a proposed adoption will be subject to criminal and psychological background checks that a normal person would consider appropriate to the purpose.

9.6.1.2. Probationary Period
Households that adopt minors will be monitored by the relevant State organs to ensure the minors are being treated fairly. The period of time during which the State conducts such monitoring activity is called the "probationary period." The probationary period lasts twelve (12) calendar months from the date the adoption is formally approved. If the responsible adults fail in their duty to the adopted children, the children will be returned to State care.

9.6.2. Eligibility to Adopt
This subsection defines who is eligible to adopt children.

9.6.2.1. Household Eligibility to Adopt
Any household registered in accordance with section 8 of this code is eligible to adopt minor children.

9.6.2.2. Individual Eligibility to Adopt
Any individual adult is eligible to adopt minor children. The adoption process will include registration of the adult and child/children as a household.

9.6.2.3. Foreign Eligibility to Adopt
Adults or families resident in countries outside the jurisdiction of the State may apply to adopt minors who are in the care of the State. The probationary period may be waived or adjusted to reflect practical reality, at the discretion of the court.

10. Contracts
This section concerns contract law.

10.1. Legal Contract
A contract is legally binding when all the following conditions are true:

  • The contract is written in electronic form or on paper
  • All signatories to the contract are of majority age
  • The terms of the contract are in compliance with law
  • The contract includes language defining how the agreement may be modified
  • The contract includes language defining how the agreement may be broken or terminated
  • The contract includes language defining how disputes are to be resolved (e.g., arbitration, mitigation, suit, or mutual agreement)

10.2. Cooling-Off Period
Any signatory to a contract may nullify the contract within three (3) calendar days of signing for any reason or for no stated reason.

10.3. Performance
All signatories to a contract will fulfill their obligations under the contract to the best of their ability and will notify the other signatories should they become unable to perform.

10.4. Unequal Power
In cases of contract disputes between signatories of vastly unequal wealth and power, the State is required to seek justice in a way that does not result in the financial devastation of individuals.

11. Labor
This section concerns the rights and responsibilities of workers and employers. The State encourages employers to treat workers fairly on their own initiative, so that excessive legal statutes need not be created to guarantee fair treatment.

11.1. Intent of Labor Law
The spirit of the law is to allow the free market to operate naturally within guardrails to protect the human rights and legal rights of workers and to ensure employers have fair access to labor.

11.2. Right to Work
All citizens and all residents within the jurisdiction of the State who have work authorization are free to seek employment without special conditions such as mandatory labor union membership or mandatory use of or membership in employer-owned facilities or organizations.

11.3. Labor Contracts and Work Agreements
This section concerns work agreements between employers and workers. Employment is subject to section 3.4.4.6 of this code, providing that the majority of employees in any enterprise operating within the State's jurisdiction must be citizens.

11.3.1. Employment Agreements Are Contracts
An employment agreement is a type of contract and is subject to section 10 of this code.

11.3.2. Term of Employment
An employment agreement may stipulate a specific time period or a time limit for engagement of the employee; or it may be open-ended with respect to time. An employment agreement may specify deliverables or performance of specific tasks rather than time limits.

11.3.3. Termination With Cause by the Employer
An employer may terminate an employment agreement if the employee fails to perform the duties of the job adequately.

11.3.4. Termination by the Employee
An employee may terminate an employment agreement according to the terms of the agreement; e.g., by giving a period of "notice," if the agreement so stipulates.

11.3.5. Parties to the Employment Agreement
The employee may sign an employment agreement on behalf of him/herself or as an agent of his/her own incorporated business entity.

Clarifying note: As the State handles healthcare, retirement, and guaranteed minimum income, and income tax rules are the same for all individuals, there is no practical difference between hiring full-time employees, part-time employees, workers through a third-party agency, or independent contractors. This is in contrast to the system of employment in the United States prior to the establishment of the State.

11.3.6. Temporary Labor Suppliers
A business entity may place its employees on temporary assignment to other companies, if that is the nature of its agreements with employees.

11.3.7. Employment Transfer Rule
A business entity whose work force comprises less than the percentage of citizens specified in section 3.4.4.6 of this code will have one calendar year following the establishment of the State to adjust their work force to bring it into compliance. There will be no exceptions due to claims that qualified citizens are not available. This is a correction to the practice in the United States of hiring large numbers of foreign workers, and is considered relevant to the Defense pillar of independence.

11.4. Labor Unions
This section concerns worker organization, work slowdown, work stoppage, and strikes.

11.4.1. Worker Organization
Workers may form organizations to help them assert and defend their rights.

11.4.2. Pay Continuance
Employers must pay striking workers their normal pay rate during strikes.

11.4.3. Limitations on Worker Activism
Workers may strike, stop work, or slow down work individually or through their labor organizations with the following limitations:

  • Employers may engage temporary replacements for striking workers; workers and labor organizations must not interfere with such workers
  • The total time workers strike or stop work cannot exceed eighty (80) hours per year of time that would otherwise have been spent on the job
  • No activity of any kind, including worker activism, is permitted to interfere with or subvert the Three Pillars of Independence


Violations may result in termination of employment.

11.5. Wages and Salaries
This section concerns compensation for work.

11.5.1. Minimum Wage
A mandatory minimum wage will be set by the Decision Making Council. The minimum wage will be expressed as an hourly pay rate. For employees who are paid a salary, the minimum annual salary will be ((standard work weeks per year) + (standard paid vacation weeks per year)) x (standard weekly work hours) x (hourly minimum wage).

Example: If the standard number of work weeks per year is 48 and the standard vacation weeks per year is 4 and the standard number of work hours per week is 32 and the minimum hourly wage is $21.00, then the minimum annual salary is (48 + 4) x 32 x 21 = $34,944.

11.5.2. Categories of Work
Each employment position will be defined to belong to a particular category of work. The categories will be defined by the Decision Making Council.

11.5.3. Wage and Salary Caps
Each category of work will have a defined minimum and maximum level of compensation based on a multiplier of the minimum wage. The multipliers will be defined by the Decision Making Council.

Clarifying note: Cliodynamics studies have found a correlation between out-of-control growth in the rich/poor gap and the decline and fall of civilizations, empires, and nation-states. The purpose of wage and salary caps is to prevent this outcome. Note that in the United States prior to the establishment of the State, some corporate executives received compensation in excess of 1,000 times the average pay of their own employees.

Example: If the salary cap for executives is 50 times the minimum wage, and we assume the same numbers as in the example under section 11.5.1, then the maximum annual salary for a Chief Executive Officer would be $1,747,200.

11.5.4. Vacation Pay
Employers must pay full-time hourly workers and salaried workers their normal pay rate for vacation time. The Decision Making Council will determine the standard number of vacation weeks per year. Employers may offer additional paid vacation time as a benefit of employment. Employers need not pay contract workers or temporary "scab" workers vacation time or other benefits.

11.5.5. Taxation of Additional Forms of Compensation
Employers may offer any benefits for employees they wish as an incentive to attract workers. The following categories will be considered taxable income:

  • leased or purchased vehicle for personal use
  • reimbursement of fuel costs for travel to and from work
  • housing allowance in excess of 50% of actual housing costs (includes utilities and services)


Other benefits will not be treated as taxable income unless a normal person would reasonably consider them to be in the nature of "income". Specific cases must be tested in court in response to formal legal challenges.

If offered, the following employer-provided benefits will not be taxable to the employee, and their costs will be deducted from the employer's tax liability:
  • supplemental healthcare
  • childcare services, either on-premises or subsidized
  • public transportation passes
  • housing allowance up to 50% of actual costs (includes utilities and services)
  • hazard pay for dangerous occupations
  • parking for personal vehicles at work locations
  • employer-provided or employer-paid recreational and fitness facilities
  • uniforms and safety gear required for the job

11.5.6. Day Work
For purposes of this statute, "day work" means labor performed without a long-term employment contract, such as general labor by workers who are picked up in the morning at a gathering place and taken to a work site.

11.5.6.1. Day Work Agreement
Employers must register day workers using an electronic or paper system. They must pay day workers at least the legal minimum wage and report compensation for income tax purposes.

11.5.7. Piece Work
For some types of work, people prefer to be paid by the quantity of their production. This may be done through a long-term employment agreement or on a day work basis. In either case, employers are responsible for registering workers and tracking their compensation for tax purposes.

11.5.8. Tips
The minimum wage system is intended to render obsolete the need for workers in certain occupations to depend on tips. Tipping is optional. Tips are not considered taxable income.

Clarifying note: The spirit of the law is to help workers in jobs that have traditionally required tips just for survival income. The minimum wage is intended to provide a living wage. Tipping can become an optional "thank you" for good service. The State does not want to penalize this sort of social behavior by taxing it. However, the door is opened for fraud. In cases where "tipping" is used as a cover to pay for illegal activities, the penalties for those illegal activities will be doubled.

11.6. Working Hours
A standard number of working hours per week will be defined by the Decision Making Council. Workers engaged for this number of hours will be considered "full time" employees. Individuals may agree to work more hours, or may accept multiple part-time jobs that amount to more hours.

Clarifying note: As most benefits are paid for through income taxes rather than by employers, there is little practical difference between a full time and part time employee. The definition of full time serves two purposes: (a) a guideline to help people judge how much work is too much, for their own health, and (b) a baseline for calculating the minimum hourly wage, especially as that number is then used to calculate the minimum annual salary for non-hourly occupations.

11.7. Worker Safety
Worker safety regulations will be defined by industry-specific standards organizations. Businesses engaged in activities regulated by such organizations are expected to comply with their regulations. Violations may be civilly and/or criminally prosecuted.

11.8. Humane Conditions
Working conditions for all occupations must be such that a normal person would consider them reasonable in context. The State will investigate all alleged violations and take appropriate action, including action against fraudulent reporting of abuses.
