Legal Rights Statutes

3.4. Legal Rights
The Legal Code recognizes the legal rights enumerated in the Constitution.
CR: 5.3
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.

3.4.1. In case of real or perceived conflict between an enumerated Human Right and an enumerated Legal Right, the intent of the Human Right will prevail. This is the Primacy of Human Rights Statute.
CR: 5.3
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.1.
BG: Backgrounder on Crime and Punishment, Section 1.1.

3.4.2. Everyone is entitled to the protection of the law and to all rights governing the interaction of law enforcement personnel and the courts with individuals.
CR: 5.3.1.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.1.

3.4.3. Everyone has the duties pertaining to the interaction of law enforcement personnel and the courts with individuals.
CR: 5.3.1.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.1.

3.4.4. Law enforcement personnel are authorized to detain, arrest, and question suspects. Law enforcement must be able to show reasonable cause for an arrest.
CR: 5.3.2.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.2.

3.4.4.1. Suspects under the age of majority cannot be interviewed without the presence of a responsible adult. The responsible adult may be any of (a) a parent, (b) a step-parent, (c) a foster parent, (d) a legal guardian, (e) an attorney retained privately, or (f) an attorney provided by the State.

3.4.5. A suspect who has been detained or arrested must either be (a) charged with a crime or (b) released within twenty-four (24) hours of their detention or arrest.
CR: 5.3.2.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.2.

3.4.6. Everyone is prohibited from harassing anyone for any reason, including but not limited to debt collection, suspicion of crime, political disagreement, or private investigation.
CR: 5.3.3.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.3.

3.4.7. Everyone is prohibited from torturing anyone for any reason, including but not limited to punishment for an offense, eliciting a confession, obtaining information, or enjoyment. "Torture" is to be construed as a normal person would ordinarily understand it, and not based on a carefully-crafted definition intended to allow torture under some other name. "Rendition," or moving the object of torture outside the normal operating realm of authorities with the purpose of avoiding prosecution under this Statute (such as the government taking a terror suspect to another country, or a law enforcement agency taking a suspect to an abandoned warehouse) will significantly increase the penalty for this offense.
CR: 5.3.4.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.4.

3.4.8. Everyone is presumed innocent of a given offense unless and until their guilt is proven in court through standard legal procedures. Exception: Between the time of arrest and the time of trial, if law enforcement deems a suspect to be a danger to public safety or to a particular individual, the suspect may be held to await trial.
CR: 5.3.5.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.5.

3.4.8.1. Corollary: Consistent with Statutes 3.3.11, 3.3.12, and 3.3.13, a suspect is deemed "innocent" as of the time law enforcement officers may enter their property or home to conduct a search or arrest. Therefore, the State must compensate the individual for property damage and for theft that was enabled by the officers' damaging normal security facilities such as door locks and gates. Even if the suspect is ultimately convicted, they were "innocent" as of the time of the search.

3.4.8.2. Corollary: The practice of "bail," or charging a fee for the release of a suspect awaiting trial, is considered a form of corruption. Either the authorities can support the claim that the suspect is a danger to the public or they cannot.

3.4.9. Everyone charged with an offense has the right to timely adjudication of their case. The first hearing or the trial must be conducted within two (2) months of the date charges were brought. If no hearing or trial has been conducted by this time, the suspect must be released, if they are being held to await trial. The first hearing or trial must then take place within four (4) months of the expiration of the first waiting period. If after that time, no hearing or trial has been conducted, the charges are automatically dropped.

For all Category 1 offenses and for Category 2 offenses in which the damage exceeds $5,000 in value, the accused is entitled to a trial by jury. For all other offenses, the accused has the right to demand a trial by jury. Such demand cannot be refused. All other cases will be adjudicated by a judge.
CR: 5.3.6.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.6.

3.4.10. All hearings and trials at every level will be open to the public, unless the Government determines the public release of evidence to be shown and/or testimony to be given may undermine the Defense Pillar.
CR: 5.3.7.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.7.

3.4.10.1. Corollary: The State must publish information about court schedules in a form readily accessible to the general public.

3.4.11. Everyone who is convicted of an offense is entitled to fair punishment. Fair punishment is determined by weighing the factors specified in subsections of this Statute in the order they are listed.
CR: 5.3.8.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.8.
BG: Backgrounder on Crime and Punishment, Sections 2, 3, and 4.

3.4.11.1. The Category of the Offense.
The State defines three Categories of offenses:

  1. 1. Egregious
  2. 2. Serious
  3. 3. Minor
The nature of the harm done to the victim(s) determines the Category of the offense.

3.4.11.1.1. Category 1: Egregious
Any offense that constitutes a violation of Human Rights or that undermines the Three Pillars of Independence is considered a Category 1 offense.

The baseline punishment range, prior to applying additional tests, is:
Minimum: Five (5) years incarceration and compensation/restitution for the victim(s), if the court deems it appropriate and if such compensation/restitution is realistic.
Maximum: Thirty (30) years incarceration and compensation/restitution for the victim(s), if the court deems it appropriate and if such compensation/restitution is realistic.
Full Citizens are reduced to Basic Citizenship status for the duration of their incarceration plus the longer of (a) twelve calendar months or (b) the waiting period determined by the court at time of sentencing.
Non-Citizens will be removed from the country after serving their sentence.

3.4.11.1.2. Category 2: Serious
Any offense that constitutes a violation of Legal Rights or that involves physical violence, theft of property, damage or destruction of property, damage to the victim's reputation, financial loss above the amount of six standard work weeks' pay at the minimum wage as of the date of the offense, or interference in the victim's practical ability to exercise their individual liberty according to Kindergarten Wisdom is considered a Category 2 offense.

The baseline punishment range, prior to applying additional tests, is:
Minimum: Compensation/restitution for the victim(s) to the extent the offender is able to provide such.
Maximum: Five (5) years incarceration plus compensation/restitution for the victim(s).
Full Citizens are reduced to Basic Citizenship status for the duration of their incarceration plus the longer of (a) twelve calendar months or (b) the waiting period determined by the court at time of sentencing.
Non-Citizens may be removed from the country after serving their sentence, at the discretion of the State and considering the recommendation of the court.

3.4.11.1.3. Category 3: Minor
Offenses that do not fall into Category 1 or 2 are considered Category 3 offenses.

The baseline punishment range, prior to applying additional tests, is:
Minimum: Warning.
Maximum: Compensation/restitution for the victim(s) to the extent the offender is able to provide such.
Citizenship status does not change.

3.4.11.2. Initial Punishment Level.
The initial punishment is set at the time charges are brought, based on law enforcement's assessment of the nature of the harm done, and is set somewhere within the minimum and maximum punishment range for the given Category of offense. For violations of Statutes that specify a punishment range, the initial punishment will be set within that range. For other offenses, the Category of the offense will determine the range of punishments.

3.4.11.3. Adjusted Punishment Level
The punishment may be increased or decreased based on the following tests. The tests are listed in the order in which they must be performed. The intent of the law is that each test in the series has a smaller potential effect on the adjusted punishment level than the test before it.

3.4.11.3.1. Test 1: Extent of Harm Done
The extent of the harm done in the offense is determined by the number of victims who were harmed and/or the amount of property damage that was done. The punishment level may be raised, at the discretion of the court, if more than one (1) victim was affected or a large amount or high value of property was damaged.

3.4.11.3.2. Test 2: Age of the Victim(s)
If any victim was younger than the age of majority at the time of the offense, the punishment level may be raised, at the discretion of the court.

3.4.11.3.3. Test 3: Offender's Intent
The offender's intent may lower the punishment level, at the discretion of the court.

  • The offender intended harm: No change in punishment.
  • The offender intended to carry out the act, but did not intend to harm anyone (negligence): Possible reduction in punishment.
  • The offender did not intend to carry out the act, but acted carelessly (negligence): Possible reduction in punishment
  • The offender did not intend to carry out the act, and did nothing to increase the risk (accident): Probable reduction in punishment, possible dismissal of the case

3.4.11.3.4. Test 4: Mitigating Circumstances
Mitigating circumstances may lower the punishment level, at the discretion of the court.

  • The offender had to choose between two courses of action that were both illegal, and chose with the intent to minimize harm: Probable reduction in punishment, possible dismissal of the case.
  • The act was unavoidable in the moment, but the situation arise in whole or in part through the offender's negligence: Reduction in punishment is unlikely, but not impossible.
  • The act was unavoidable - genuinely accidental: Probable reduction in punishment, possible dismissal of the case.

3.4.11.3.5. Test 5: Offender's Criminal History
If the offender has a history of repeated offenses, and/or is considered a danger to the public, the punishment level may be increased at the discretion of the court. If the offender has no criminal history a reduction in punishment level is possible, at the discretion of the court.

3.4.11.3.6. Test 6: Offender's Attitude
If the offender has exhibited a cooperative, humble, and remorseful attitude through the investigation and trial, a reduction in punishment is possible, at the discretion of the court. If the offender has exhibited an arrogant, aggressive, belligerent attitude through the investigation and trial, an increase in punishment is possible, at the discretion of the court.

3.4.11.3.7. Test 7: Character Witness Testimony
In trial, the prosecution and the defense may call character witnesses, and individuals may volunteer as character witnesses without being called, and courts must allow them to testify. The court may consider this testimony to increase or reduce the punishment level, at its discretion.

3.4.11.3.8. Test 8: Offender's Age
If the offender was a minor at the time of the offense, the punishment may be reduced, at the court's discretion.

3.4.11.3.9. Test 9: Offender's Mental State
If the offender was not in a normal mental state at the time of the offense, and this was a factor in the offense, the punishment may be reduced, at the court's discretion.

3.4.11.3.10. Test 10: Court's Judgment
Judges and other professionals who work in the criminal justice system on a daily basis develop a sense of how to read people. The court's impressions of the offender may affect the level of punishment.

3.4.11.3.11. Test 11: Precedent
After applying all other tests, the court will compare the resulting punishment with punishments levied in similar cases in the past to assure general consistency.

3.4.12. Everyone who is arrested and/or charged with an offense is entitled to professional legal representation. An individual may engage the services of the attorney of their choice, or ask the State to provide an attorney, or may choose to represent themselves.
CR: 5.3.9.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.9.

3.4.13. No one is required to answer questions, or to speak at all, when detained or arrested by law enforcement.
CR: 5.3.10.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.10.

3.4.14. Everyone is entitled to honesty in interactions between law enforcement or other authorities and individuals. Both law enforcement and the individual are expected to engage honestly, should they choose to speak at all. An individual may be prosecuted for dishonesty in their interactions with law enforcement, and law enforcement may be prosecuted for dishonesty with individuals (for example, to trick them into divulging information or making a confession).
CR: 5.3.11.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.11.

3.4.15. No one is required to incriminate themselves in interviews with law enforcement or in trials. Even if an individual waives their right to keep silent, they need not say anything self-incriminatory.
CR: 5.3.12.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.12.

3.4.16. Everyone is entitled to compensation for wrongful treatment by law enforcement. When detention, arrest, false charges, charges that are not proven in court, or visible law enforcement activity around the home or workplace of a suspect result in material damage such as job loss, emotional harm such as spousal abandonment, or reputational damage, the wronged individual may seek compensation through the court system.
CR: 5.3.13.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.13.

3.4.17. In case of wrongful conviction, the individual affected or their survivors are entitled to compensation from the State for time served, actual lost income, and potential lost income.
CR: 5.3.14.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.14.

3.4.18. Everyone who is incarcerated is entitled to Human Rights and just treatment in general. Law enforcement personnel and other employees of the prison system are prohibited from harassing or harming inmates. They must also protect inmates from other inmates. Inmates are entitled to food, water, basic clothing, basic healthcare, reasonable protection from temperature extremes, and a clean place to sleep. The punishment for their offense consists of spending time in prison, and nothing beyond that.
CR: 5.3.15.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.15.

3.4.19. If an inmate behaves violently and must be subdued by force, they will be considered to have exercised the free choice of reckless action. Law enforcement personnel will not be liable for the harm, unless it is later shown that they abused their authority and did not need to use force.
CR: 5.3.15.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.15.

3.4.20. Anyone who is convicted of an offense is entitled to appeal the conviction. The appeal must be filed within two (2) years of the date of conviction. If the appeal fails, the individual must wait a minimum of one (1) year before filing another appeal, and they must do so within two (2) years of that date. This process may be repeated up to a maximum of three (3) times.
CR: 5.3.16.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.16.

3.4.21. When an individual is acquitted of a charge, the individual may not be charged again with the same instance of the same offense. This is the Double Jeopardy Statute.
CR: 5.3.17.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.17.

3.4.22. If the court at the time of sentencing allows for parole, the opportunity to apply for parole must not be denied.
CR: 5.3.18.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.18.

3.4.23. Everyone is entitled to due process of law. If it is determined that correct procedures were not followed in handling a case, the charges may be dismissed by a court and the suspect released. To avoid abuse of this Statute by focusing on the letter of the law ("case dismissed on a technicality"), if law enforcement can show they acted in good faith and made their best effort to follow procedures without illicit intent, they can argue for the case to remain open or, if the case has been dismissed, to be re-opened.
CR: 5.3.19.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.19.

3.4.24. Everyone who completes a prison sentence or who is determined to have been falsely convicted is entitled to be released immediately. It is an abuse of human rights for law enforcement to "play games" by delaying releases. If law enforcement acted in good faith, no charges will be brought. If law enforcement intentionally delayed the release for any reason, the recommended punishment is for those responsible for the delay to serve a sentence in the same facility and population as the former inmate of a length equal to the length of the delay.
CR: 5.3.20.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.20.

3.4.25. The Right of Protest
CR: 5.3.21.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.21.

3.4.25.1. Citizens are entitled to protest policies or actions of the Government or of any public or private sector organization in public and/or by means of technology.
CR: 5.3.21.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.21.

3.4.25.2. Non-Citizens are not entitled to protest.
CR: 5.3.21.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.21.

3.4.25.3. Actions that harm people or damage property, either in person or in cyberspace, are not considered "protest" and are not protected.
CR: 5.3.21.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.21.

3.4.25.4. Actions taken in the name of "protest" that undermine the Three Pillars of Independence are not protected.
CR: 5.3.21.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.21.

3.4.25.5. Protests may take place in whatever location(s) they will be effective, in both physical space and cyberspace.
CR: 5.3.21.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.21.

3.4.25.6. Advance planning and a permit may be required for protest actions involving more than 100 people in physical space.
CR: 5.3.21.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.21.

3.4.26. The Right of Petition
CR: 5.3.22.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.22.
Citizens may petition the Government to change this Legal Code. See Statute 1.5.1.

3.4.26.1. Citizens are entitled to petition the Government for any reason.
CR: 5.3.22.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.22.

3.4.26.2. Non-Citizens are not entitled to petition the Government.
CR: 5.3.22.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.22.

3.4.26.3. Signature Points.
Petitions that have certain purposes must meet a threshhold number of signature points to be considered.

  • Full Citizen with higher education: Signature is worth 3 points
  • Full Citizen with basic education: Signature is worth 2 points
  • Basic Citizen: Signature is worth 1 point
CR: 5.3.22, [TBD].
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.22.

3.4.27. Everyone is entitled to have a home country and to hold citizenship in that country. Citizens of America can never be stripped of their Citizenship for any reason.
CR: 5.3.23.
BG: Backgrounder on Human Needs, Human Rights, Legal Rights, Privileges, Duties, and Freedom, Section 4.2.23.

3.4.27.1. Corollary: Should a Citizen voluntarily renounce their Citizenship as a form of protest, the State will not recognize the renunciation. The Citizen will continue to enjoy the rights and have the duties of a Citizen. Should a foreign government that does not recognize dual citizenship require an American to renounce their citizenship in order to live in the foreign country, the State will continue to recognize the American's Citizenship. The State may issue a document verifying they have received the renunciation, to satisty an administrative procedure of the foreign government, but in fact the American will always be a Citizen and may return to America at any time with no loss of rights.
