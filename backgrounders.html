Backgrounders

Clauses in the Constitution and statutes listed in the Legal Code are brief. This is a matter of structure and style for documents of those types, and we do not want to clutter them with lengthy explanations of intent. At the same time, we want to avoid interpretations that go astray of the original intent of a Constitutional clause or a statute. This has been an ongoing problem in the US, as people have wildly-varying interpretations of the US Constitution.

To that end, we provide essays that describe and explain the philosophical underpinning and/or rationale for each broad theme addressed in the Constitution and the Legal Code. They often include hypothetical or historical examples to illustrate our intent. These essays are called Backgrounders because they provide background information in support of the brief statements in the primary documents.

Backgrounders are intended to have nearly the same legal force as the Constitution and the Legal Code. They are to be consulted and generally followed when considering changes to legislation and adjudication of court cases. The Backgrounders have more weight than legal precedent has in the State system. This is intentional, and is a correction to a problem with the present system in the US, in which a bad precedent can continue to cause harm to people in case after case for many years.
